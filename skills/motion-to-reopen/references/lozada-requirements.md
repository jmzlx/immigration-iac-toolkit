# Lozada Requirements: Complete Legal Framework

## Matter of Lozada, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988)

The foundational case for ineffective assistance of counsel (IAC) claims in immigration law. The BIA established a three-part test that applicants must satisfy.

### The Three Lozada Requirements

#### 1. Deficient Performance
The applicant must show that counsel's conduct fell below the standard of practice expected of immigration attorneys operating in similar circumstances.

**Standard**: This is an objective test. The question is not whether the client is unhappy with the strategy, but whether the conduct was objectively unreasonable given the facts and law available at the time.

**What constitutes deficiency**:
- Failure to file required forms or meet deadlines
- Failure to advise on critical statutory provisions (e.g., §245(k) limitations)
- Providing materially incorrect legal advice
- Failure to explain material consequences of a course of action
- Neglecting to explore available alternatives
- Failure to communicate with the client about case strategy
- Misrepresenting the applicant's eligibility or options

**What does NOT constitute deficiency**:
- Disagreement with trial strategy or litigation choices
- A different approach than what the client would have preferred
- A decision that in hindsight appears unwise
- Losing a meritorious case if the representation was otherwise competent

**For Julia's case**:
- Deficiency: Counsel failed to advise that filing I-485 while accruing unlawful presence violated §245(k); failed to explain "portability" does not maintain lawful status for adjustment purposes; failed to advise about consular processing alternative with EB1-A
- This is strong deficiency because it involves misunderstanding or failure to explain a core statutory requirement

#### 2. Prejudice
The applicant must demonstrate that the deficient performance prejudiced the case—that is, there is a reasonable probability that, but for the error, the result would have been different.

**Standard**: The test is not whether the applicant would definitely have won. Rather, the question is whether there is a reasonable probability of a different outcome. The outcome does not have to be approval; it could be a different type of relief or a different procedural route.

**How to show prejudice**:
- Identify the alternative outcome that would have occurred but for the error
- Show that the alternative was actually available at the time
- Provide evidence that the applicant would have chosen/pursued the alternative
- Demonstrate that the alternative outcome would likely have been more favorable

**For Julia's case**:
- **Deficient conduct**: Failure to advise on §245(k) and consular processing
- **Alternative outcome**: Consular processing with her EB1-A employer
- **Why available**: EB1-A was being processed; employer was supporting it; no statutory bar to consular processing for EB1-A beneficiaries
- **Why Julia would have pursued it**: She had no choice—she couldn't legally adjust on I-485 once she knew about §245(k)
- **Why it's more favorable**: Consular processing avoids the §245(k) bar entirely; she would have processed as an approved EB1-A beneficiary
- **Strongest evidence of prejudice**: Her EB1-A was approved, proving she met the substantive test all along. The deficient counsel advice was the sole barrier.

#### 3. Procedural Compliance
The applicant must follow strict procedural rules for filing IAC claims. These rules protect the integrity of the system and ensure due process for the respondent (the former counsel).

**Requirements under 8 CFR §103.5(a)(4)**:
- The motion must be filed within the time period specified (generally 30 days from denial, but see exceptions below)
- The applicant must submit an affidavit detailing the facts upon which the claim is based
- The applicant must notify the former counsel in writing and provide an opportunity to respond
- If the applicant has filed a complaint with the state bar association, proof of filing must be included
- If no bar complaint has been filed, the motion should explain why (if applicable)

**For Julia's case**:
- File Form I-290B (not I-290A) within 30 days of I-485 denial
- Submit affidavit explaining the errors and reliance on counsel
- Serve former counsel by certified mail (no less than 15 days before filing or concurrent with filing)
- Provide proof of service
- File (or explain reason for not filing) state bar complaint

## Subsequent Developments: Compean, Bangaly, and Others

Matter of Lozada established the framework, but subsequent cases refined it.

### Matter of Compean, 24 I&N Dec. 710 (BIA 2009)

The BIA clarified that ineffective assistance claims apply to both immigration judges (in removal proceedings) and USCIS (in adjustment proceedings). The Lozada test applies uniformly.

**Key holding**: There is no heightened burden or different standard based on the forum (IJ vs. USCIS). The three-part test is consistent across both settings.

**Implication for Julia**: This motion to reopen before USCIS (not in removal proceedings) is properly filed under Lozada. The standard does not change because it's USCIS rather than an immigration court.

### Matter of Bangaly-Jalloh, 23 I&N Dec. 606 (BIA 2003)

Addressed when counsel can be held liable for a client's misunderstanding if counsel provided correct advice but the client failed to understand or remember it.

**Rule**: Counsel must not only provide correct information but must ensure the client understands the material consequences. If a client testifies that they were not properly advised on a critical point, and there is no contemporaneous documentation that they were told, the court may find the testimony credible and find deficiency.

**Implication for Julia**: If she can show via email, calendar, or testimony that counsel never explained §245(k) or "portability" issues, that's deficiency even if counsel claims they explained it. Email evidence is particularly valuable here.

### In re J-E-C-, 23 I&N Dec. 368 (BIA 2002)

The BIA held that a claim of ineffective assistance must be supported by a showing that counsel's conduct was not merely different from what the applicant preferred, but fell below a standard of competence.

**Standard articulated**: The "prevailing professional norms" test requires that counsel's conduct be evaluated against what a competent immigration attorney would have done in similar circumstances, not against some theoretical standard of perfection.

**Implication for Julia**: Counsel's failure to advise on §245(k) must be shown to violate prevailing professional norms. This is strong because §245(k) is a foundational statute in adjustment law that any competent EB1-A counsel should know and explain.

## Circuit-Specific Standards for IAC Claims

While the Lozada test applies in immigration courts and before USCIS, the federal circuit courts have developed variations when reviewing IAC claims on appeal.

### Second Circuit (New York)
The Second Circuit applies the Lozada test but has noted that the prejudice requirement requires showing a "reasonable probability" of a different outcome, not mere possibility. See Matter of Compean (BIA decisions citing circuit law).

**Relevance**: If Julia's case could be appealed to the Second Circuit, the motion should be careful to demonstrate more than a theoretical possibility that consular processing would have been approved—it should show reasonable probability.

### Ninth Circuit (California, Western US)
The Ninth Circuit has been more permissive in finding IAC and has emphasized that counsel must explain material consequences of actions. It has also recognized that affidavits from the applicant about what they were told are generally admissible and credible.

**Relevance**: If applicable, the Ninth Circuit standard may be slightly more favorable to Julia's motion, particularly regarding reliance on her affidavit about what she was and was not told.

### Other Circuits
The Eleventh Circuit and others apply similar standards but may weight procedural compliance more heavily. Check the applicable circuit for Julia's case.

## The Filing Package Checklist

This is the procedural foundation. Missing any element can result in dismissal.

### Required Documents

#### 1. Form I-290B (Motion to Reopen)
- **Version**: Always check USCIS.gov for the current version. Using an outdated form can cause delays.
- **Part A**: Indicate "Motion to Reopen" and mark the box for "Ineffective Assistance of Counsel"
- **Part B**: Brief statement of facts (expand in the motion brief)
- **Fee**: Currently $650 as of 2024 (verify current amount; fees change)
- **Service center address**: Ensure you're filing to the correct USCIS service center that issued the original denial

#### 2. Motion Brief
- 5–15 pages typical length (check any local rules)
- Three-part structure: Deficiency + Prejudice + Procedural Compliance
- Legal citations to Lozada, applicable circuit law, and statutory provisions
- Detailed factual statement supporting each element
- See motion-template.md for detailed structure

#### 3. Applicant Affidavit
- Sworn statement (notarized, though USCIS sometimes accepts unnotarized)
- Describe your understanding of your case at the time
- Describe what you were told (or not told) by former counsel
- Describe what you relied on
- Describe the harm caused by the errors
- Describe your discovery of the errors and the misleading advice
- For Julia:
  - "I was never advised that filing I-485 would result in accrual of unlawful presence"
  - "I was told that portability maintained my status, so I could safely adjust"
  - "I was never told that consular processing was an option"
  - "I only learned of the §245(k) problem after the I-485 was denied"
  - "When I later pursued the EB1-A, it was approved, showing I was always qualified"

#### 4. Affidavit from Former Counsel (or Demand)
- **If available**: Request former counsel to provide an affidavit responding to your allegations. They may clarify what they did or did not advise.
- **If unavailable or refused**: Include proof of your request (letter sent by certified mail) and a statement explaining that they did not respond. USCIS can proceed without their affidavit.
- **Strategic note**: If former counsel admits the errors, your case is much stronger. If they deny the errors, your own affidavit and documents (emails) become critical.

#### 5. Proof of Bar Complaint
- **Timing**: File the bar complaint before or concurrent with the motion to reopen.
- **Content**: Include specific allegations of deficient performance tied to your case
- **Proof**: Include the bar association's confirmation of receipt or case number
- **Address**: Include proof of where/when you filed and with which bar association
- **Why required**: Lozada requires evidence that you are pursuing bar discipline. Some cases have been dismissed for failure to do this.

#### 6. Evidence of Notification
- **Requirement**: Under 8 CFR §103.5(a)(4), you must notify former counsel and give them at least 15 days to respond before filing (or file concurrently).
- **Method**: Certified mail with return receipt is standard. Keep the receipt.
- **Content**: Include a copy of the motion brief and this notice, informing them they have 15 days to respond
- **Proof**: Attach the certified mail receipt to your filing package as an exhibit

#### 7. Supporting Evidence of Deficiency
Organize and label these documents:
- All email exchanges with former counsel
- Client letters or memos from counsel (if any)
- Retainer agreement or engagement letter
- Prior USCIS notices or correspondence
- Calendar of events showing when you were given advice
- Any notes or internal memos documenting what counsel said
- For Julia:
  - Emails discussing the I-485 filing strategy
  - Any discussion of §245(k) (or lack thereof)
  - Emails about "portability"
  - Timeline showing when counsel advised "go ahead and adjust while I-140 is pending"

#### 8. Supporting Evidence of Prejudice
- Approved EB1-A I-140 (strongest evidence of merit)
- Timeline showing EB1-A was in process at time of I-485 filing
- Proof of accrued unlawful presence (I-94 history, stamps, work authorization records)
- Evidence that consular processing was available:
  - Affidavit from EB1-A employer confirming they would have cooperated
  - Copies of consular processing procedures from DOS website
  - Any prior communications about consular processing option
- Calculation showing §245(k) violation: How many months of unlawful presence? When did it reach 24+ months?
- For Julia: Evidence that she would have immediately pursued consular processing if advised

#### 9. Exhibit List and Certificate of Service
- **Exhibit list**: Numbered list of all documents attached, with brief description
- **Certificate of Service**: Statement that you served copies on USCIS, former counsel, and state bar association, with dates and methods
- **Format**: See motion-template.md for proper formatting

## Form I-290B: Detailed Guidance

The official USCIS form for motions to reopen. This is NOT the same as I-290A (Motion to Reconsider).

### Key Differences: I-290B vs. I-290A

| Aspect | I-290B (Reopen) | I-290A (Reconsider) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Basis | New evidence, fraud, material error of fact, ineffective assistance of counsel | Only legal errors in the original decision |
| When to use | Attorney mistakes, new evidence discovered | Wrong interpretation of law applied to same facts |
| For IAC claims | YES—use this form | NO—do not use this |
| Time limit | Generally 30 days, but "new evidence" grounds may have longer window | More restrictive timing |
| Scope | Can result in de novo review | Limited review |

**For Julia**: You are filing I-290B because of ineffective assistance of counsel (one of the enumerated grounds under 8 CFR §103.5(a)).

### How to Complete Form I-290B

**Part A—Information About You**:
- Applicant name
- A-number (from I-485)
- Date of birth
- Contact information

**Part B—Information About Decision**:
- Check "Motion to Reopen" (not Motion to Reconsider)
- Date of decision (I-485 denial date)
- Decision detail (e.g., "I-485 denied due to accrual of unlawful presence under INA §245(k)")

**Part C—Reason for Motion**:
- Check the box for "Ineffective Assistance of Counsel"
- If there is also new evidence, check that box as well
- If the decision contained fraud, check that box

**Part D—Statement in Support**:
- Brief narrative (do not exceed the space; expand in the motion brief)
- "I was not properly advised about the §245(k) implications of my I-485 filing. My attorneys failed to explain that I was accruing unlawful presence. They failed to advise me about consular processing. My subsequent EB1-A I-140 was approved, proving I qualified all along. But for the deficient representation, I would have pursued consular processing and avoided the §245(k) bar."

**Part E—Signature**:
- Sign and date the form
- If filing through a representative, have them sign as well (or include power of attorney)

## 8 CFR §103.5: Regulatory Framework

This is the regulation that governs motions to reopen and reconsider. Understanding it is critical.

### 8 CFR §103.5(a)—Motion to Reopen

**§103.5(a)(1)**: A motion to reopen must be filed within 30 days of the decision, except:
- (i) If the motion is based on new evidence, it must be filed within 30 days of when the evidence became available
- (ii) If the motion is based on changed law or policy, it can be filed anytime (limited circumstances)
- (iii) In removal proceedings, a motion to reopen may be filed if based on fraud or ineffective assistance of counsel

**§103.5(a)(2)**: A motion to reopen shall state the new facts to be proved and shall be supported by affidavits or other documentary evidence.

**§103.5(a)(3)**: A motion to reopen shall not be granted unless the evidence is material and was not available or, with the exercise of due diligence, could not have been discovered in time to be presented at the initial hearing or interview.

**§103.5(a)(4)**: In cases where ineffective assistance of counsel is alleged:
- The motion must be accompanied by an affidavit detailing the claim
- The applicant must notify the former counsel in writing of the claim
- Former counsel must be afforded an opportunity to respond
- If a bar complaint has been filed, proof must be provided; if not, the reasons should be explained
- The motion must specify what competent counsel would have done

**§103.5(a)(5)**: A motion to reopen is not applicable in the case of denial or approval of an application or petition made by USCIS where the decision is made solely on the basis of documentary review without a hearing.

**§103.5(a)(6)**: One motion to reopen or one motion to reconsider must be sufficient. A second motion shall not be filed unless it is based on new evidence and the original motion did not address the evidence.

### Implications for Julia's Motion

- **Timing**: Filed within 30 days of I-485 denial (or request extension immediately if past 30 days)
- **New evidence**: Even if past 30 days, the motion may be filed if based on "new evidence" (discovery of counsel's errors counts as new evidence)
- **Affidavit**: Must include her affidavit detailing the IAC claim
- **Notice to counsel**: Formal notice and opportunity to respond
- **Bar complaint**: Proof of bar complaint filing or explanation
- **One bite at the apple**: Only one motion to reopen allowed under this claim (but if new evidence is discovered, a second motion may be filed)

## Time Limits for Filing

### The 30-Day Rule
The motion must generally be filed within 30 days of receiving the I-485 denial notice.

**Starting point**: The date of the written decision, not when you received it or when you discovered the problem.

**Extension**: If past the 30-day window, immediately file:
1. A Motion to Reopen based on "new evidence" (discovery of the counsel's errors qualifies)
2. Accompanied by a request for enlargement of time under 8 CFR §103.5(a)(1)(ii)

**Document the discovery**: Include evidence of when you discovered the attorney errors:
- Date you learned about §245(k)
- Date you discovered the approved EB1-A
- Correspondence showing when the problems became apparent

### Special Circumstances
- **Fraud**: If the former counsel committed fraud (affirmative misrepresentation), longer time limits may apply
- **Newly discovered evidence**: The 30-day clock runs from when evidence is discovered, not from the original decision
- **Removal proceedings**: Different rules apply; check 8 CFR §103.5 for specific language

For Julia, if she has just discovered the problems, argue that the 30-day period should run from the discovery date.

## USCIS vs. Immigration Court Jurisdiction

### Motions Before USCIS (Julia's Case)
- **Timing**: 30 days from denial
- **Venue**: File at the USCIS service center that issued the denial
- **Standard**: Lozada three-part test
- **Effect of granting**: USCIS reopens the I-485 and reconsiders on the merits
- **Appeal**: If motion is denied, appeal is limited; may be subject to judicial review

Julia's motion is being filed before USCIS because her I-485 denial has not resulted in removal proceedings. This is the correct forum.

### Motions Before Immigration Court
- If Julia had been placed in removal proceedings based on the I-485 denial, she would file the motion before the immigration judge
- **Timing**: More flexible; can be filed before the judge at any time
- **Standard**: Same Lozada test
- **Effect of granting**: Judge reopens the hearing and reconsiders

Julia's case is not in immigration court (no removal proceedings initiated), so USCIS is the proper forum.

## Julia's Case: Key Arguments and Evidence

### The Deficiency Argument

**What counsel should have done**:
1. Explained INA §245(k): A beneficiary who has accrued 24 months or more of unlawful presence is barred from adjusting status
2. Explained the consequence: Filing I-485 while accruing unlawful presence would eventually trigger this bar
3. Discussed the timeline: EB1-A was in process; no need to rush I-485
4. Explained "portability": USCIS portability rules under INA §204(j) allow an EB1-A beneficiary to change jobs and maintain I-140 priority date, but this does NOT maintain lawful status for adjustment purposes
5. Advised about consular processing: If unlawful presence was a concern, the EB1-A could be processed through consular processing (no I-485 needed)

**Evidence of deficiency**:
- Emails showing counsel advised to "go ahead and adjust while I-140 is pending" without discussing §245(k)
- Emails discussing "portability" as a solution without clarifying it doesn't maintain status for adjustment
- Timeline showing no discussion of consular processing option
- Affidavit: "I was never told about §245(k) or that I was accruing unlawful presence. I was told that portability would protect my status."

**Why it's deficient**:
- §245(k) is foundational to adjustment law
- Any competent EB1-A counsel must know and explain it
- Failure to advise is not a strategy choice; it's negligence
- Misrepresenting "portability" is affirmative error

### The Prejudice Argument

**The but-for scenario**:
- If counsel had explained §245(k) and the unlawful presence timeline, Julia would have immediately consulted with her employer about consular processing
- Consular processing for EB1-A is a standard, viable alternative
- She would have pursued CP instead of I-485
- As an EB1-A beneficiary processing through CP, she would have been approved without the §245(k) bar

**Evidence of prejudice**:
1. **Strongest**: Approved EB1-A I-140 proves she was eligible all along
2. **Timeline**: Show when EB1-A approval occurred and that it could have been processed via CP at that time
3. **Employer affidavit**: "We would have supported consular processing if advised of the §245(k) issue"
4. **Calculation**: Show that accrual of unlawful presence reached 24+ months at X date, causing the bar
5. **Alternative outcome**: But-for the deficient advice, she would have adjusted as an EB1-A without the §245(k) bar

**Why prejudice is strong**:
- The approved EB1-A is not speculative—it's a decided fact
- Consular processing was available and viable
- The entire I-485 filing became problematic because of counsel's failure to advise on §245(k)
- She had a substantively viable path (EB1-A CP) that counsel failed to explore

### The Procedural Compliance Argument

**Step 1: Bar complaint**
- File with state bar association in the state where counsel was licensed
- Include specific allegations of deficient performance
- Keep proof of filing

**Step 2: Notify former counsel**
- Send by certified mail with return receipt
- Include copy of motion and statement that they have 15 days to respond
- Keep proof of delivery

**Step 3: Gather procedural documents**
- Form I-290B completed
- Applicant affidavit signed and sworn
- Proof of bar complaint filing
- Proof of counsel notification (certified mail receipt)
- All supporting evidence organized as exhibits

**Step 4: File within time limit**
- Count 30 days from I-485 denial
- File at correct USCIS service center
- Include all required documents
- Keep copy of receipt/proof of filing
