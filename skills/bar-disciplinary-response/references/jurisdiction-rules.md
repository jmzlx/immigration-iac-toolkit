# Professional Conduct Rules by Jurisdiction

This reference covers the specific professional conduct rules applicable to bar complaints in DC, NY, NJ, MA, and FL. Use this to cite jurisdiction-specific authority in your responses to bar disciplinary proceedings.

## DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA

### Applicable Rules of Professional Conduct

**Rule 1.1: Competence**
- Attorney must provide competent representation (legal knowledge, skill, preparation, diligence)
- For specialized matters (immigration law), attorney need not be specialist if brings "necessary learning and skill"
- Immigration law is sufficiently specialized that failure to bring adequate knowledge = incompetence

**Rule 1.3: Diligence**
- "Act with reasonable diligence and promptness" in representing client
- Diligence = regularity of attention and frequency of activity appropriate to the matter
- Mere passage of time ≠ lack of diligence if attorney is advancing the case

**Rule 1.4: Communication**
- "Keep client reasonably informed" about status and means of representation
- Must explain material matters sufficiently to permit informed decisions
- Client right to information ≠ right to daily updates on every development
- Attorney may batch communications if no urgent material developments

**Rule 1.16: Declining or Terminating Representation**
- If attorney withdraws, must give reasonable notice and return client property
- Relevant if complaint alleges abandonment or sudden termination

**Rule 5.3: Responsibilities Regarding Nonlawyer Assistants**
- Attorney responsible for work of paralegals and support staff
- If paralegal made errors, attorney is liable unless proper supervision was in place
- Relevant if complaint alleges delegated work was deficient

**Rule 8.4: Misconduct**
- Conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, misrepresentation = violation
- Criminal conduct prejudicial to fitness to practice = violation
- Used broadly in complaints to catch conduct not fitting other rules

### DC Grievance Process

1. **Filing**: Complaint filed with DC Bar Counsel's office
2. **Initial Screen**: Bar Counsel reviews for facial sufficiency (usually within 30 days)
3. **Respondent Answer**: Attorney has 20 days to respond (check individual notice—can be shorter if expedited)
4. **Investigation**: Bar Counsel investigates; may request documents, take statements
5. **Recommendation**: Bar Counsel issues recommendation: dismiss, investigate further, or ethics hearing
6. **Hearing**: If ethics hearing ordered, both parties present evidence before 3-person panel
7. **Appeal**: Respondent can appeal panel decision

**Key Deadline**: Responses in DC are often 20 days; some notices grant extensions upon request.

## NEW YORK

### Applicable Rules of Professional Conduct

**Rule 1.1(a): Competence**
- Attorney must act with competence in legal representation
- For immigration law, failure to know immigration law = failure to meet Rule 1.1
- Competence includes understanding BIA and circuit court precedent on immigration relief

**Rule 1.3(a): Diligence**
- "Represent a client zealously and pursue the client's lawful objectives"
- "Reasonable diligence" is contextual; applies to attorney's actions and pace, not external delays
- DHS processing delays, INS adjudication, court scheduling = not attorney's fault unless attorney caused delay
- Attorney need not expedite matters beyond control of the attorney

**Rule 1.4: Communication**
- Substantially similar to DC and other jurisdictions
- Attorney must keep client reasonably informed
- No requirement for daily, weekly, or even monthly updates absent material developments

**Rule 8.4(h): Misconduct**
- Conduct prejudicial to fitness to practice as lawyer = violation
- Broader catch-all than other jurisdictions; used for general professional negligence

### NY Grievance Process

1. **Filing**: Grievance filed with New York State Bar's Grievance Committee (sometimes routed by county)
2. **Respondent Answer**: Attorney has 30 days to answer (extendable upon written request)
3. **Investigation**: Grievance Committee investigates; may appoint hearing officer
4. **Options**: Dismiss, caution letter, admonition, or disciplinary hearing
5. **Hearing**: If hearing ordered, formal proceeding with transcript
6. **Discipline**: Ranging from admonition to suspension/disbarment
7. **Appeal**: Appellate Division review available

**Key Deadline**: 30 days for response in NY (often extendable); check individual notice.

## NEW JERSEY

### Applicable Rules of Professional Conduct

**RPC 1.1: Competence**
- "A lawyer shall provide competent representation"
- Attorney "shall not intentionally, recklessly, or negligently" fail to provide competent service
- "Competent representation" = legal knowledge, skill, preparation, diligence
- RPC 1.1(b): For specialized areas (immigration), attorney may rely on specialist counsel if proper coordination and supervision

**RPC 1.3: Diligence**
- "A lawyer shall act with reasonable diligence and promptness"
- Promptness = timeliness; diligence = attention to case
- Contextual: what's diligent in fast-paced litigation ≠ diligent in administrative immigration appeals

**RPC 1.4: Communication**
- Very similar to other jurisdictions
- Must "keep a client reasonably informed about the status of a matter"
- "Material information" = outcomes, material deadlines, material developments
- Routine updates on procedural matters = not material unless client requested specific updates

**RPC 8.4: Misconduct**
- Similar catch-all to other jurisdictions
- Adds: "conduct that adversely reflects on fitness to practice" = violation

### NJ Grievance Process

1. **Filing**: Complaint filed with NJ Office of Attorney Ethics (OAE)
2. **Intake**: OAE does preliminary review; can dismiss for lack of jurisdiction or insufficient allegations
3. **Respondent Answer**: Attorney has 30 days to file answer (often extendable upon request)
4. **Investigation**: OAE investigates; may subpoena documents and take statements
5. **Recommendation**: OAE recommends dismissal or formal ethics hearing
6. **Hearing**: If ethics hearing, three-attorney panel presides
7. **Decision**: Panel may dismiss, reprimand, suspend, or disbar
8. **Appeal**: Supreme Court review available

**Key Deadline**: 30 days for response in NJ (check individual notice for deadline).

## MASSACHUSETTS

### Applicable Rules of Professional Conduct

**Rule 1.1: Competence**
- Attorney must possess "legal knowledge and skill" reasonably necessary for representation
- Preparation = knowledge of law and facts relevant to matter
- For immigration law: knowledge of BIA, circuit court precedent, current statutory framework

**Rule 1.3: Diligence and Commitment**
- "Act with commitment and dedication to the interests of the client"
- "Pursue objectives of the client" unless objectives unlawful or attorney has valid ground to withdraw
- Contextual: criminal defense diligence ≠ administrative law diligence

**Rule 1.4: Communication**
- "Keep a client informed of matters material to the representation"
- Must explain material matters adequately to enable client decision-making
- MA adds: attorney can determine timing and form of communication absent express agreement to contrary
- "Material information" = outcomes, material developments, not all procedural steps

**Rule 8.4: Misconduct**
- "Conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice" = violation
- Similar catch-all

### MA Grievance Process

1. **Filing**: Complaint filed with Board of Bar Overseers (BBO)
2. **Respondent Answer**: Attorney has 30 days to file answer
3. **Investigation**: BBO staff investigates; standard is "probable cause" that violation occurred
4. **Screening Panel**: If probable cause found, case goes to screening panel
5. **Hearing**: If screening panel finds issue for discipline, formal hearing before hearing officer
6. **Discipline**: BBO can dismiss, issue admonition, reprimand, suspend, or disbar
7. **Appeal**: To Supreme Judicial Court

**Key Deadline**: 30 days for response in MA.

## FLORIDA

### Applicable Rules of Professional Conduct

**Rule 4-1.1: Competence**
- Attorney must "provide competent representation"
- "Competence" = legal knowledge, skill, preparation, diligence
- Attorney must know immigration law or associate with someone who does

**Rule 4-1.3: Diligence**
- "Act with reasonable diligence"
- Reasonable = prompt action on matters requiring prompt attention
- Delays caused by opposing party, court, or government = not attorney's lack of diligence

**Rule 4-1.4: Communication**
- "Keep a client reasonably informed"
- "Timely disclosure" of material information
- FL adds specificity: "Reasonable notice of the material matters affecting the client's legal rights"
- Rule 4-1.4(b)(1): "Reasonable notice" ≠ instant notice; timing depends on materiality and urgency

**Rule 4-8.4: Misconduct**
- Conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, misrepresentation, or conduct prejudicial to fitness
- Similar to other jurisdictions

### FL Grievance Process

1. **Filing**: Complaint filed with Florida Bar's Grievance Section
2. **Respondent Answer**: Attorney has 30 days to file answer (extendable upon request)
3. **Investigation**: Grievance staff investigates; reviews documents, takes statements
4. **Standard**: "Probable cause" that violation of professional conduct occurred
5. **Case Evaluation**: If probable cause found, case escalated to discipline process
6. **Hearing**: Disciplinary hearing before referee (or court-appointed judge)
7. **Discipline**: Ranges from admonition to disbarment
8. **Appeal**: To Florida Supreme Court

**Key Deadline**: 30 days for response in FL; check individual notice for exact date.

## Immigration Law Specific Considerations

### Matter of Lozada Standard
All five jurisdictions apply the Lozada three-prong test to ineffective assistance complaints:
1. Deficient performance (breach of duty)
2. Causation (deficiency caused harm)
3. Prejudice (actual harm to client's immigration case)

**Key Point**: Your response must address all three prongs. Rebutting only prong 1 (deficient performance) is insufficient. You must also show no causation or no prejudice.

### Immigration Competence Elements
For immigration law specifically, competence requires:
- Knowledge of current immigration statutes (INA, 8 U.S.C. § 1101 et seq.)
- Knowledge of BIA precedent and circuit court decisions on forms of relief
- Knowledge of DHS/USCIS procedures and processing times
- Knowledge of possible consequences of guilty pleas, admissions, etc.
- Knowledge of collateral consequences in immigration law

If attorney lacked this knowledge, all five jurisdictions will find incompetence.

### Diligence in Immigration Context
Immigration cases differ from other practice areas:
- Government processing times are unpredictable (can range from weeks to years)
- Court schedules are often set by government, not attorney
- Deadlines can be strict (appeals, I-90 renewal, NTA response) or flexible (I-140 review)
- Diligence ≠ speed; diligence = regular attention and timely filing where required

Emphasize these contextual factors in your response.

## Case Law References for Immigration IAC

### Key BIA Decisions
- **Matter of Lozada**, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988)—establishes three-prong test
- **Matter of Assadullah**, 21 I&N Dec. 1 (BIA 1995)—attorney ineffectiveness in asylum cases
- **Matter of Compean**, 24 I&N Dec. 710 (BIA 2008)—prejudice requirements in criminal-immigration IAC
- **Matter of M-J**, 24 I&N Dec. 586 (BIA 2008)—causation requirement (would relief have been granted?)

### Circuit Court Decisions
- **US v. Kaczynski**, 239 F.3d 1108 (9th Cir. 2001)—Lozada framework applied in deportation context
- **Jury v. State**, 865 P.2d 1072 (Or. 1994)—standard for "reasonably competent immigration attorney"
- **Quinteros-Soriano v. INS**, 26 F.3d 907 (9th Cir. 1994)—attorney's duty to advise on consequences

### Why These Matter
These cases establish that:
1. Immigration IAC has a higher bar than general criminal IAC
2. Causation requires showing "but for" counsel's error, relief would have been granted
3. Prejudice requires actual harm to immigration status/deportation exposure
4. Competence in immigration law is specialized skill

Cite these in your response when rebutting Lozada prongs.

## Common Complaint Allegations and Strategic Responses

### Allegation: "Attorney failed to explore all forms of relief"
**Response Strategy**:
- Show attorney considered [Relief A] and [Relief B]
- Explain why [Relief B] was not viable (statutory bars, facts, etc.)
- Cite case law showing attorney's strategic choice was within professional judgment
- Address prejudice prong: would [Relief B] have been granted if pursued? (often no)

**Citation**: DC Rule 1.1; NY Rule 1.1(a); NJ RPC 1.1; MA Rule 1.1; FL Rule 4-1.1

### Allegation: "Attorney did not explain consequences of guilty plea"
**Response Strategy**:
- Produce written notice of immigration consequences given to client
- Show client's own testimony or admission re: understanding
- Cite Sixth Amendment case law on plea colloquy sufficiency
- Address prejudice: would client have rejected plea if consequences explained? (burden on complainant to show)

**Citation**: All jurisdictions' Rule 1.4; also cite federal criminal procedure collateral consequences cases

### Allegation: "Attorney missed deadline"
**Response Strategy**:
- Explain what deadline (appeal, I-90, NTA response, etc.)
- If attorney caused miss, explain impact (was it prejudicial? could appeal for good cause?)
- If client/government caused miss, show attorney took prompt corrective action
- Address prejudice prong carefully: what would have happened if deadline not missed?

**Citation**: DC Rule 1.3; NY Rule 1.3(a); NJ RPC 1.3; MA Rule 1.3; FL Rule 4-1.3

### Allegation: "Attorney was not responsive to client"
**Response Strategy**:
- Produce email chain showing responsiveness
- Define "responsive" (emails within 48 hours? returned calls same day? not possible in all contexts)
- Distinguish routine inquiries from urgent matters
- Show attorney was responsive to material matters

**Citation**: DC Rule 1.4; NY Rule 1.4; NJ RPC 1.4; MA Rule 1.4; FL Rule 4-1.4

### Allegation: "Attorney's strategy was flawed"
**Response Strategy**:
- Explain strategy and reasoning
- Show strategy was within range of professional judgment
- Cite case law supporting strategic choice
- Address prejudice: was outcome actually worse due to strategy choice?

**Citation**: DC Rule 1.1 and 1.2(e); all jurisdictions' competence rules

## Template Language for Responses

### Opening
"The Respondent Attorney respectfully submits this response to the Complaint filed on [date] by [Complainant Name]. The allegations in the Complaint mischaracterize the scope and quality of representation provided to [Client Name]. As demonstrated below, the Respondent Attorney's conduct complied with the [DC/NY/NJ/MA/FL] Rules of Professional Conduct and met all applicable standards of care."

### Addressing Deficient Performance
"The Complaint alleges that the Respondent Attorney failed to [allegation]. This allegation is factually and legally insufficient. Under [Jurisdiction] Rules of Professional Conduct [Rule], competent representation requires [standard]. The Respondent Attorney met this standard because [factual explanation]. As demonstrated in Exhibits [X], [Y], [Z], [client] received [outcome]. [Additional sentence tying to professional conduct rule.]"

### Addressing Causation
"Even if the Respondent Attorney's conduct fell below the applicable standard—which the Respondent Attorney respectfully contends it did not—the Complaint fails because [Client Name] cannot demonstrate causation. The Lozada standard requires showing that 'but for' the attorney's deficient conduct, the result would have been different. Here, [explanation of why outcome would have been same regardless]. As a result, the causation prong of Lozada fails."

### Addressing Prejudice
"The Complaint also fails the Lozada prejudice requirement. Under immigration law, prejudice requires showing actual harm to [Client Name]'s immigration status or deportation exposure. Here, [explanation of why no prejudice occurred]. [Client Name] has not demonstrated any actual harm resulting from the Respondent Attorney's conduct, if any deficiency existed."

### Closing
"For the foregoing reasons, the Respondent Attorney respectfully requests that the Complaint be dismissed for failure to establish a violation of the [Jurisdiction] Rules of Professional Conduct. The Respondent Attorney committed no breach of duty to [Client Name], and the allegations are not supported by the documentary evidence or applicable legal standards."
