# Motion to Reopen (Ineffective Assistance of Counsel)

## Why This Matters

Your case was denied due to attorney errors, not merit. Your approved EB1-A proves you were always qualified. A Motion to Reopen under Matter of Lozada is your legal mechanism to undo an unjust denial and get back to the substantive decision your case deserves.

Without this motion, a wrongful denial becomes final. With it, you can prove ineffective assistance and reset the clock. This is not about second-guessing strategy—it's about correcting fundamental attorney failures that violated your rights.

## The Lozada Framework: What You Must Prove

Matter of Lozada established a three-part test for ineffective assistance of counsel claims in immigration:

1. **Deficient Performance**: Your attorneys' conduct fell below prevailing professional norms. This is not "could have been better"—it's "objectively unreasonable given the circumstances."

2. **Prejudice**: But for their errors, you would have received a different outcome. This requires showing what would have happened if they'd acted properly.

3. **Procedural Compliance**: You must file correctly under 8 CFR §103.5, including affidavits, proof that you notified your former attorneys, and evidence of any bar complaints.

For Julia's case, all three elements are met:
- **Deficiency**: Failure to advise about §245(k) implications and misleading advice about "portability"
- **Prejudice**: If properly advised, she would have pursued consular processing, avoiding the accrual of unlawful presence
- **Procedure**: Following all Form I-290B and notification requirements

## What You're Assembling: The Complete Filing Package

A Motion to Reopen package for USCIS must include:

### Core Documents
- **Motion Brief**: Your legal argument (5–15 pages typically)
- **Affidavit from Applicant**: Your sworn statement of facts and reliance on counsel
- **Affidavits from Former Counsel** (if available) or proof of demand
- **Bar Complaint Filing Proof**: Evidence that you filed with state bar association
- **Evidence of Notification**: Proof you gave former attorneys notice and chance to respond
- **Form I-290B**: Official USCIS form for motions to reopen (do not confuse with I-290A for reconsideration)
- **Fee Payment**: Currently $650 (verify current amount)

### Supporting Evidence
- All original I-485 documentation
- Your approved EB1-A I-140 (proves substantive eligibility)
- Email exchanges showing attorney communications and errors
- Calendar/timeline showing when you learned of problems
- Any written advice from former counsel that contradicts what they now claim
- Prior USCIS decisions or correspondence
- Evidence of accrued unlawful presence (I-94, stamps, employment records)
- Proof that consular processing was available and viable

## Building Your Argument: Three-Part Structure

### Part A: Counsel's Performance Was Deficient

Show that your attorneys' conduct was objectively unreasonable. Use:
- **Substantive knowledge gaps**: Failed to advise on §245(k) implications; didn't understand EB1-A timeline
- **Contradictory advice**: Claimed "portability" maintained status when it did not
- **Failure to explore alternatives**: Never mentioned consular processing option
- **Documentation**: Email chains showing what they did and didn't tell you

**Why this matters**: USCIS will scrutinize whether errors were truly unreasonable. Don't claim mere negligence—show they violated established immigration law principles.

### Part B: Prejudice (The "But-For" Causation)

This is crucial. Show the alternative outcome:
- **If properly advised**: "Had counsel explained §245(k), I would have immediately consulted my employer about consular processing"
- **Timeline**: EB1-A was in process; no accrual needed if consular route taken
- **Employer support**: If your EB1-A employer would have cooperated with consular processing, document that commitment
- **Substantive eligibility**: Your approved EB1-A proves you met the test all along

**Why this matters**: Prejudice requires showing a "reasonable probability" of a different result. Your EB1-A approval is your strongest prejudice evidence.

### Part C: Procedural Compliance

Prove you followed the rules under 8 CFR §103.5:
- ✓ Filed within proper timeframe (generally 30 days from denial notice; seek extension if needed)
- ✓ Included applicant affidavit explaining reliance on counsel
- ✓ Notified former attorneys in writing with proof of service
- ✓ Filed bar complaint or explain why not (unavailable? statute of limitations?)
- ✓ Included evidence of the deficient performance
- ✓ Used correct form (I-290B, not I-290A)

**Why this matters**: Procedural errors can sink a meritorious claim. This is checklist work—do it perfectly.

## Drafting the Motion Brief: Key Sections

### Introduction
State your case clearly:
- "This is a Motion to Reopen filed under 8 CFR §103.5(a)(4) and Matter of Lozada based on ineffective assistance of counsel."
- Identify your approved EB1-A I-140 as context
- Name your former counsel and the errors they made

### Facts
Use chronological order:
1. I-485 filing date and classification
2. Counsel's specific actions (or failures to act)
3. When you discovered the problem
4. The I-485 denial
5. The subsequent EB1-A approval (proving merit)

### Legal Standard
Cite Matter of Lozada directly. Reference Strickland v. Washington for the two-part test (deficiency + prejudice) if appropriate. Note that circuit courts may have stricter standards—research your applicable circuit.

### Argument: Deficiency
Quote or paraphrase the emails/communications showing what your attorneys failed to explain. Contrast their advice against established immigration law. Use citations to INA §245(k) and EB1-A case law.

### Argument: Prejudice
Build the "but-for" timeline:
- IF counsel had advised properly on §245(k)
- THEN you would have pursued consular processing with your EB1-A employer
- RESULT: No unlawful presence accrual; I-485 approval

Connect your approved EB1-A as proof that substantive eligibility existed all along.

### Argument: Procedural Compliance
Briefly confirm you've met all Lozada requirements.

### Relief Requested
"Reopen the I-485 petition and issue an approval notice."

### Certificate of Service
List everyone you're serving (USCIS, former counsel, bar association).

## Critical Procedural Requirements

### Timing
- Generally **30 days** from the I-485 denial notice
- If you're past 30 days, request an extension under 8 CFR §103.5(a)(1)(ii) (motion to reopen is always available if based on new evidence; precedent change is harder)
- IMPORTANT: Consult a current immigration attorney about timing and whether any exceptions apply

### Form I-290B (Motion to Reopen)
- **DO NOT use I-290A** (Motion to Reconsider). That's for legal error in the decision itself, not for new evidence.
- I-290B is specifically for new evidence, fraud, or material matters affecting the decision
- Ineffective assistance of counsel counts as a ground under I-290B
- Check USCIS.gov for current filing fee and address for your service center

### Service and Notification
You must serve:
1. **USCIS Service Center** that issued the denial (use certified mail, keep proof)
2. **Former Counsel** by certified mail (they have 15 days to respond under 8 CFR §103.5(a)(4))
3. **State Bar Association** (file complaint if you haven't; if already filed, include proof)

Failure to serve former counsel properly can derail the motion.

### State Bar Complaint
Before or concurrent with filing the motion, file a bar complaint with your state bar association. Include:
- Specific failures (didn't advise on §245(k), misrepresented "portability," didn't explore consular processing)
- Dates and sequence of events
- Copies of relevant communications
- Impact on your case (denied I-485)

**Why this matters**: USCIS views a bar complaint as evidence that you took your claim seriously. It also creates external accountability.

## Demonstrating Prejudice: The "But-For" Analysis

Prejudice is the hardest part. You need to show a reasonable probability that, but for the error, you'd have a different outcome.

For Julia:
- **Error**: Not advised of §245(k) implications; misled on "portability"
- **Impact**: Accrued 24+ months of unlawful presence, which bars I-485
- **But-for scenario**: If advised, she would have immediately notified her EB1-A employer and initiated consular processing instead of filing I-485
- **Evidence**:
  - Timeline showing EB1-A employer was supporting the case
  - Affidavit from employer confirming willingness to cooperate on consular processing
  - Documentation that consular processing was available and timely
  - Copy of approved EB1-A showing she qualified all along

The strongest evidence of prejudice is the approved EB1-A. It proves:
1. She met the substantive standard
2. There was a viable alternative path (consular processing)
3. The attorney error directly caused the problematic I-485 filing

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Filing the wrong form**: I-290A (Motion to Reconsider) is for legal error, not attorney mistakes. Use I-290B.

2. **Incomplete service**: Not notifying former counsel properly can result in dismissal. Use certified mail with return receipt.

3. **Weak prejudice argument**: Saying "I would have done something different" is not enough. Show the specific alternative path and that it was viable.

4. **Missing procedural elements**: No bar complaint, no affidavit, no proof of notification = likely dismissal.

5. **Conflating with appeal**: This is not an appeal. You're not asking USCIS to reconsider their legal reasoning. You're arguing the original decision was tainted by attorney incompetence.

6. **Overstating the error**: Focus on errors that actually changed the outcome. If the I-485 would have been denied anyway, IAC won't help.

7. **Not getting the EB1-A approved first**: You need that approval as proof of merit. Without it, prejudice is much harder to show.
