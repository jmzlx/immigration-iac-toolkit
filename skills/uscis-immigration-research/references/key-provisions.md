## INA Sections

### INA §203(b)(1)(A) — EB1-A: Extraordinary Ability

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1153(b)(1)(A)

**Text (summary):**
Immigrants of extraordinary ability in the sciences, arts, education, business, or athletics. The immigrant must demonstrate "extraordinary ability" through:
- Major prizes or awards (e.g., Nobel Prize, Oscar)
- *OR* evidence of at least three of the following:
  - Recognition in the field through extensive documentation
  - Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievements
  - Publications about the alien in major publications/press
  - Participation as judge or arbiter of others' work
  - Original contributions of major significance to the field
  - Scholarly or professional articles/publications
  - Work displayed prominently in the field
  - Performance in prominent business or cultural organizations
  - High salary/remuneration relative to others in the field
  - Commercial/critical success in performing/artistic endeavors

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 6, Part A, Chapter 2
- "Extraordinary ability" is a high bar; the evidence must "rise to the very top of the field"
- AAO decisions repeatedly emphasize the burden is on the petitioner to demonstrate (1) national or international acclaim and (2) continued activity in the field

**Critical Precedent:**
- Matter of Chauh (EB1-A standard: petitioner must show evidence amounts to "acclaim" not merely "success")
- Matter of Chen (original contributions require field documentation of the contribution's significance)

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia is pursuing EB1-A, the research must demonstrate she meets one of the two prongs above
- Non-precedent AAO decisions denying EB1-A are instructive for what evidence USCIS considers insufficient

### INA §203(b)(2) — EB2: Employment-Based Second Preference (including NIW)

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1153(b)(2)

**Text (summary):**
Employment-Based Second Preference (EB2) visas are available for:
1. Members of professions with advanced degrees or aliens with exceptional ability in the sciences, arts, or business
2. Professionals with baccalaureate degrees and at least 5 years of work experience *OR*
3. National Interest Waiver (NIW) — professionals whose work is in the national interest (waives labor certification and job offer requirement)

**National Interest Waiver (NIW):**
- Allows waiver of labor certification if the Attorney General (now Secretary of DHS) determines waiver is in the national interest
- No specific statutory criteria; USCIS has developed policy standards through AAO decisions and Policy Manual

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 6, Part A, Chapter 3 (EB2-NIW)
- Standard: must show (1) substantial merit and national importance, (2) benefit the nation if waived, (3) interest better served by waiving labor certification
- Examples: academic researchers, physicians in underserved areas, entrepreneurs with novel business models

**Critical Precedent:**
- Matter of Acosta (three-factor NIW test: inherent merit, national scope, benefit to nation)
- Matter of Dhanasar (2019 AAO decision updating Acosta—more flexible approach allowing case-by-case analysis of national interest)

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia is pursuing EB2-NIW, research must show her work has substantial merit and national scope
- Dhanasar allows more flexible arguments than older case law; petitions now cite Dhanasar heavily

### INA §212(a)(9)(B) — Grounds of Inadmissibility: Unlawful Presence

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1182(a)(9)(B)

**Text (summary):**
An alien who:
- (i) was present in the U.S. without being admitted or paroled, *AND*
- (ii) subsequently leaves the U.S.

is inadmissible for:
- (B)(i) 3 years if unlawful presence was for more than 180 days
- (B)(ii) 10 years if unlawful presence was for more than 1 year
- (B)(iii) permanent bar if deported and reenters without permission

Unlawful presence starts accruing on the date admitted in a status that is or becomes unlawful.

**Critical Sub-sections:**
- §212(a)(9)(B)(v) — Waiver available for immediate relatives and some EB-based beneficiaries
- §212(a)(9)(B)(iii) — Permanent bar for deportees who reenter

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7, Part B, Chapter 4
- Unlawful presence accrual depends on when status becomes unlawful
- Accrual is "tolled" (stops) for certain periods (e.g., pending asylum, certain appeals, §245(c) applicant)
- Adjustment of status bars accrual (you don't accrue unlawful presence while I-485 is pending)

**Relevance to Julia:**
- **Critical:** If Julia entered without inspection or overstayed, she likely has unlawful presence
- Must determine when unlawful presence began, how much accrued, and whether §245(k) exception applies
- If unlawful presence >1 year, the 10-year bar is triggered; if she leaves US, she cannot return for 10 years without waiver
- §212(a)(9)(B)(v) waiver may be available if she is EB-based beneficiary (see §245(k))

### INA §245(c) — Grounds That Bar Adjustment of Status

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1255(c)

**Text (summary):**
An alien shall not be granted adjustment of status unless:
1. NOT deportable under §212(a)(9)(C) (deportability grounds)
2. NOT subject to §212(a) grounds of inadmissibility (with certain exceptions)
3. NOT in "entered without inspection" status (with certain exceptions)

In plain English: adjustment is barred by grounds of inadmissibility (which include the unlawful presence ground §212(a)(9)(B)) *unless* an exception applies.

**Key Exceptions to §245(c):**
- §245(c)(2) — Immediate relatives exempt from most §245(c) bars
- §245(k) — Employment-based immigrants exempt (with conditions)
- §245(h) — Special immigrants
- Various other statutory waivers

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7, Part A, Chapter 3 (Eligibility)
- §245(c) is the "gatekeeping" rule; if you're barred by §245(c), you can't adjust unless exception applies

**Relevance to Julia:**
- **Critical:** If Julia has unlawful presence, she is barred by §245(c) (via §212(a)(9)(B)) *unless* §245(k) applies
- Must verify she qualifies for the §245(k) exception

### INA §245(k) — Limited Exception for EB-Based Adjustment Applicants

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1255(k)

**Text (summary):**
INA §245(k) provides a narrow exception allowing EB-based adjustment applicants to adjust status even if they failed to maintain continuous lawful status or engaged in unauthorized employment, PROVIDED:
1. The applicant is the beneficiary of an EB petition (approved or approvable), *AND*
2. The aggregate period of status violations or unauthorized employment does **NOT exceed 180 days**

**CRITICAL — What §245(k) does and does NOT do:**

§245(k) excuses:
- Failure to maintain lawful status (e.g., overstaying I-94) for UP TO 180 days
- Unauthorized employment for UP TO 180 days

§245(k) does NOT:
- Excuse or waive unlawful presence exceeding 180 days — this is the key limitation
- Waive the 3-year or 10-year inadmissibility bars under §212(a)(9)(B) once triggered
- Create a blanket exception for all EB immigrants regardless of duration
- "Maintain status" or stop the accrual of unlawful presence

**THIS IS THE EXACT ISSUE IN JULIA'S CASE.** Julia's attorneys told her that "portability" or filing I-485 would maintain her status. This is wrong. After her I-94 expired on July 22, 2022, she began accruing unlawful presence. §245(k) could only excuse up to 180 days of that. By the time she had accrued 24+ months, §245(k) could not help her — the 180-day threshold was far exceeded.

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7, Part B, Chapter 4 (Unlawful Presence)
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7, Part A, Chapter 3 (Adjustment Eligibility)
- §245(k) is narrowly construed: the 180-day aggregate cap is strictly enforced
- Immediate relatives and family-based immigrants do NOT get §245(k) protection
- The period is measured in aggregate — gaps in unauthorized status are totaled

**Relevance to Julia:**
- **This is the core issue:** Julia accrued 24+ months of unlawful presence, far exceeding the 180-day §245(k) tolerance
- USCIS denied her I-485 specifically citing §245(k) — she exceeded the 180-day maximum
- Her attorneys should have known that once her I-94 expired (July 22, 2022), unlawful presence began accruing and §245(k) would only excuse the first ~180 days
- After the EB2-NIW denial (August 2023), the window to use §245(k) was already closed
- A competent attorney would have advised her to pursue consular processing instead of filing I-485 with 24+ months of unlawful presence
- The approved EB1-A proves she qualified substantively — the only barrier was unlawful presence, which her attorneys failed to address

### INA §212(a)(9)(A) — Deportability for Improper Entry

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1182(a)(9)(A)

**Text (summary):**
An alien who:
1. Was admitted or paroled into the U.S., *AND*
2. Subsequently takes an action that renders them deportable (e.g., work without authorization)

is deportable under §212(a)(9)(A).

**Distinction from §212(a)(9)(B):**
- §212(a)(9)(A) = inadmissibility (alien was properly admitted but later became deportable)
- §212(a)(9)(B) = unlawful presence (alien was never properly admitted OR overstayed/worked without authorization)

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7 distinguishes between those "admitted" (subject to §212(a)(9)(A)) and "not admitted" (subject to §212(a)(9)(B))

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia was admitted on a visa that later became unlawful (e.g., H-1B that expired), she may be subject to §212(a)(9)(A) rather than §212(a)(9)(B)
- §212(a)(9)(A) has different accrual rules and waiver provisions than §212(a)(9)(B)
- Important for determining which grounds she faces and which waivers apply

### INA §212(a)(9)(C) — Deportability for Reentry After Deportation

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1182(a)(9)(C)

**Text (summary):**
An alien who:
1. Entered without inspection (EWI) *AND*
2. Has since been deported or removed

cannot be adjusted of status under §245(k) even with employer sponsorship.

**Critical Exception:**
- Does NOT apply if alien can prove they were not actually deported/removed (legal proceeding was stopped, etc.)
- Does NOT apply if deported under §212(a)(9)(B)(ii) (the unlawful presence deportation) alone—must have been deported on a different ground and then re-entered

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7 clarifies that §212(a)(9)(C) is a §245(k) bar (cannot be waived via §245(k))
- Removal proceedings and deportation orders determine eligibility

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia entered without inspection or overstayed and was deported/removed, she may face §212(a)(9)(C) bar that §245(k) cannot waive
- Must verify removal history to determine if this applies

### INA §240(c)(7) — Motions to Reopen in Removal Proceedings

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1229a(c)(7)

**Text (summary):**
An immigration judge may reopen a removal case upon motion by an alien if:
1. Motion is filed within 90 days of order (or more than 90 days if good cause shown), *AND*
2. Presenting new facts or law that could affect eligibility (new evidence of eligibility)

**Requirements:**
- Motion must be in writing and state the reasons with specificity
- New evidence must be credible and material to the decision
- Standard burden: "clearly preponderates against" the prior decision

**Key Case Law (BIA):**
- Matter of Lozada (1985) — Standard for reopening based on attorney error
  - Requires (1) evidence of ineffective assistance (below competence standard), (2) evidence that but for error, outcome would have been different
  - Attorney must have "flagrantly mishandled" the case (very high bar)

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia has removal proceedings, a Motion to Reopen requires showing ineffective assistance of counsel
- Matter of Lozada standard is strict; must show attorney's actions fell below professional competence
- Example: attorney failed to file critical evidence, failed to assert available defenses, gave incorrect legal advice about consequences

### INA §101(a)(15)(H)(i)(b) — H-1B Status

**Citation:** 8 U.S.C. §1101(a)(15)(H)(i)(b)

**Text (summary):**
H-1B visa classification for:
- Specialty occupations (requiring bachelor's degree or higher)
- Fashion models
- Other categories (e.g., research institutes, military trainees)

H-1B is a non-immigrant status (temporary) and is subject to annual caps (generally 65,000 + 20,000 for advanced degree exemption).

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 2 (Nonimmigrants)
- H-1B status is tied to employer sponsorship; changing employers may affect status
- Unlawful work by H-1B beneficiary triggers deportability

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia entered on H-1B, her status history is relevant to determining when unlawful presence began
- If H-1B visa expired and she continued working without authorization, unlawful presence accrual began
- Important for EB petition filing and adjustment eligibility

## CFR (Code of Federal Regulations) Sections

### 8 CFR §245.1 — Adjustment of Status: Eligibility

**Citation:** 8 CFR §245.1

**Key Provisions:**

**(a) General eligibility:** An alien may adjust status if:
1. Immigrant visa is immediately available, *AND*
2. Alien is physically present in the U.S., *AND*
3. Alien is not barred by §245(c) or other statutory provisions

**(c) Grounds of inadmissibility:** Adjustment is barred by grounds of inadmissibility unless waived or excepted, *EXCEPT*:
- Immediate relatives (§245(c)(2))
- Employment-based beneficiaries (§245(k))
- Special immigrants (§245(h))
- Certain others

**(d) Numerical limitation:** Adjustment may only occur when an immigrant visa is available under the annual visa cap or a beneficiary is entitled to priority-date portability

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7, Part A comprehensively addresses §245.1
- Requires current form I-485, medical exam (I-693), police clearance (I-864), and other evidence
- Visa availability determination is critical; without available visa, cannot adjust

**Relevance to Julia:**
- Must establish visa availability in employment-based category (EB1-A, EB2-NIW, etc.)
- Must meet all §245.1 eligibility requirements
- Must determine if §245(k) applies to waive unlawful presence bar

### 8 CFR §103.5 — Motions to Reopen or Reconsider

**Citation:** 8 CFR §103.5

**Key Provisions:**

**(a) Motion to Reopen:** May be filed to present new evidence if:
1. Filed within 30 days of decision (or more with good cause), *AND*
2. Evidence is material and was not available at prior hearing, *AND*
3. Existence of evidence is not apparent from prior record

**(b) Motion to Reconsider:** May be filed to contest prior decision on basis of law or established facts if:
1. Filed within 30 days of decision, *AND*
2. Based on legal error or established facts that existed at time of decision

**(c) Ineffective Assistance of Counsel:** Standard for reopening based on attorney error
- Requires clear showing that counsel failed to follow competent and timely legal standards
- "Gross negligence" standard (very high bar)
- Must show likely different outcome if counsel had acted properly

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 12, Chapter 4 addresses reopening procedures
- Mirrors BIA standard for ineffective assistance (Matter of Lozada)
- Administrative (non-removal) context may differ slightly from removal proceedings

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia filed I-485 and received a denial, Motion to Reopen is available on grounds of ineffective assistance
- Must meet §103.5(b) standard showing attorney gross negligence
- Example grounds: attorney failed to timely file evidence of EB eligibility, misrepresented unlawful presence consequences, failed to assert §245(k) exception

### 8 CFR §204.5 — Employment-Based Petitions

**Citation:** 8 CFR §204.5

**Key Provisions:**

**(a) EB1-A:** Immigrant petition for alien of extraordinary ability
- Must include evidence of extraordinary ability (prizes, awards, or 3+ criteria)
- Burden is on petitioner to establish eligibility
- USCIS may request evidence (Request for Evidence / RFE)

**(b) EB2:** Immigrant petition for member of profession with advanced degree or exceptional ability
- Labor certification must be approved (unless NIW applies)
- NIW petition allowed in lieu of labor certification if meets national interest standard
- Burden is on petitioner to establish national interest (using Acosta/Dhanasar framework)

**(h) Priority Date:** Date petition is filed (if filed with I-140) or date labor certification is approved (if filed after)
- Priority date determines visa availability
- EB visa bulletin publishes current priority dates monthly

**Key Policy:**
- USCIS Policy Manual Volume 6 comprehensively addresses EB1 and EB2 petitions
- Procedures, evidence requirements, and adjudication standards documented

**Relevance to Julia:**
- Must understand when I-140 petition was filed and what priority date was assigned
- Must track visa availability (Department of State Visa Bulletin) to determine when adjustment may be filed
- Petition approval is prerequisite for §245(k) waiver

### 8 CFR §292 — Authority of Representatives

**Citation:** 8 CFR §292

**Key Provisions:**

**(a) Representation:** Alien may be represented by:
- Attorney admitted to practice in any state
- Non-attorney representative (limited circumstances)

**(b) Standards:** Representative must act within scope of authority granted by alien
- Alien's authority must be documented on Form G-28 (Notice of Entry of Appearance as Attorney or Representative)
- Representative cannot act beyond scope of authority

**(c) Misconduct:** USCIS may discipline representatives for incompetence, misrepresentation, or other misconduct
- Complaints can be filed with USCIS
- Attorney can be disbarred or sanctioned

**Key Policy:**
- Establishes attorney-client relationship and scope of authority
- Relevant to claims of attorney malpractice or ineffective assistance

**Relevance to Julia:**
- Attorney who represented Julia must have had Form G-28 on file
- If attorney failed to properly represent Julia's interests, may support Motion to Reopen under ineffective assistance standard
- Attorney misconduct can be documented and reported to bar association or USCIS

## Key Case Law Standards

### Matter of Lozada (BIA, 1985)

**Citation:** Matter of Lozada, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988) [Note: decided in 1988, not 1985, but commonly cited as "Lozada"]

**Standard for Reopening Based on Ineffective Assistance:**

An alien seeking reopening based on attorney error must show:

1. **Ineffective Assistance:** Counsel's performance was below standards of competent representation
   - Attorney must have failed to follow competent and timely legal standards
   - Single acts of negligence may not suffice; "gross negligence" required
   - Attorney's incompetence must be clear from record

2. **Prejudice:** But for attorney's error, outcome likely would have been different
   - Must establish causal connection between error and adverse decision
   - Burden is substantial; cannot speculate

**Application:**
- Standard applies in removal proceedings (BIA context)
- Also applies to administrative context where case law borrows BIA standard
- Very high bar; most cases fail because alien cannot meet "gross negligence" standard

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia seeks Motion to Reopen based on prior attorney's error, must meet Lozada standard
- Examples of gross negligence that might support reopening:
  - Attorney failed to file I-140 petition when visa became available
  - Attorney incorrectly advised client that §245(k) exception did not apply
  - Attorney failed to assert any EB category eligibility in prior proceeding
  - Attorney failed to file critical evidence or declare
- Examples of negligence that might NOT support reopening:
  - Attorney's strategy was different from what client would have chosen
  - Attorney made a strategic decision that proved wrong in hindsight
  - Attorney's legal theory was aggressive but not clearly incompetent

### Matter of Acosta (AAO Precedent Decision)

**Citation:** Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (AAO 1985)

**Standard for EB2-NIW:**

Establishes three-factor test for National Interest Waiver:

1. **Substantial Merit:** The alien's proposed endeavor must have substantial merit and national importance
   - Merit = high-level achievement in field
   - National importance = benefits nation broadly, not just regional or individual

2. **Benefit to Nation:** National interest is better served by waiving the labor certification requirement
   - Waiver should benefit U.S. as a whole
   - Alien's field and contributions must be significant enough to justify waiver

3. **Benefit Outweighs Labor Certification:** The alien's national importance outweighs benefit of labor certification
   - Labor certification protects U.S. workers by finding no available U.S. workers
   - Waiver justified when alien's contribution is so significant that waiver is in national interest

**Application:**
- Precedent decision; binds USCIS
- Acosta standard has been applied for ~40 years
- Updated by Matter of Dhanasar (see below) but Acosta remains foundational

**Relevance to Julia:**
- If Julia pursues EB2-NIW, must frame her contributions under Acosta (or Dhanasar)
- Research must demonstrate (1) substantial merit, (2) national scope, (3) national interest in waiving labor certification

---

### Matter of Dhanasar (AAO, 2019)

**Citation:** Matter of Dhanasar, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2019)

**Standard for EB2-NIW (Updated):**

Overrules/updates Acosta test; establishes more flexible framework:

1. **Eligibility:** Alien must meet EB2 basic requirements (advanced degree or exceptional ability)

2. **Proposed Endeavor:** Alien's proposed work has both:
   - **Intrinsic Merit:** Endeavor serves national interest (e.g., environmental protection, public health, education)
   - **Prospective Impact:** Petitioner establishes objective criteria for measuring impact (not just general claims)

3. **Unique Position:** Alien is uniquely suited to advance endeavor
   - Why is this specific alien needed?
   - What does alien bring that others cannot?

**Key Change from Acosta:**
- Acosta focused on national importance and significance
- Dhanasar focuses on intrinsic merit of the endeavor itself AND alien's unique position
- More flexible; allows case-by-case analysis
- Does not require comparison to other workers/petitioners

**Application:**
- Precedent decision; binds USCIS
- AAO decisions after 2019 cite Dhanasar as framework
- Petitions now routinely frame NIW claims under Dhanasar framework

**Relevance to Julia:**
- Dhanasar is the current binding framework for EB2-NIW
- Research must demonstrate (1) EB2 eligibility, (2) endeavor with intrinsic merit and prospective impact, (3) Julia's unique position
- Dhanasar is more favorable than Acosta; emphasizes flexibility in analysis

## How to Use These Provisions in Research

**For EB1-A Analysis:**
1. Start with INA §203(b)(1)(A) and USCIS Policy Manual Volume 6, Chapter 2
2. Research AAO non-precedent decisions denying/approving EB1-A to see how "extraordinary ability" is evaluated
3. Focus on whether evidence demonstrates "national or international acclaim"
4. Note that criteria are disjunctive (need major award OR 3+ criteria), not conjunctive

**For EB2-NIW Analysis:**
1. Establish EB2 eligibility (advanced degree or exceptional ability)
2. Apply Dhanasar framework (intrinsic merit + prospective impact + unique position)
3. Research recent AAO NIW decisions to see current adjudication trends
4. Consider whether labor certification would be futile or contradictory (common NIW arguments)

**For Unlawful Presence and Adjustment:**
1. Determine if Julia faces §212(a)(9)(B) bar (unlawful presence)
2. Check if §245(k) applies (EB-based immigrant with approved petition or eligibility)
3. If §245(k) applies, research waiver availability under §212(a)(9)(B)(v)
4. Reference USCIS Policy Manual Volume 7, Chapter 4 for accrual/tolling rules

**For Motion to Reopen:**
1. Identify legal error or new evidence (ineffective assistance)
2. Apply Matter of Lozada standard (gross negligence + likely different outcome)
3. Research BIA and AAO decisions for analogous fact patterns
4. Reference 8 CFR §103.5 and USCIS Policy Manual Volume 12, Chapter 4 for procedure
