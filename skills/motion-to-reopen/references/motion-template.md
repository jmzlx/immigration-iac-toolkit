## I. INTRODUCTION (1–2 pages)

### Purpose
State your case clearly and concisely. This is the reader's first impression. Make it count.

### Structure
1. Identify yourself and the decision being challenged
2. State the legal basis (Lozada, ineffective assistance)
3. Preview your three-part argument
4. State the ultimate relief sought

### Example for Julia

---

**INTRODUCTION**

Julia Salas respectfully submits this Motion to Reopen under 8 CFR §103.5(a)(4) and Matter of Lozada, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988), based on the ineffective assistance of counsel. On [DATE], USCIS denied her Application to Register Permanent Residence or Adjust Status (Form I-485) on the sole ground that she had accrued 24 months or more of unlawful presence in violation of INA §245(k), rendering her ineligible to adjust status.

Ms. Salas's denial was caused not by any lack of qualification for permanent residence, but by the gross professional failures of her former immigration counsel, [FIRM NAME], specifically [ATTORNEY 1 NAME] and [ATTORNEY 2 NAME]. Her subsequent and independent pursuit of an EB-1A First Preference visa has resulted in the approval of her I-140 Immigrant Petition on [DATE], definitively proving that she is substantively eligible for permanent residence.

But for her counsel's deficient representation, Ms. Salas would have pursued consular processing with her EB-1A sponsoring employer rather than filing an I-485 application vulnerable to the §245(k) bar. Her former counsel failed to:

1. Advise her of the §245(k) limitation on adjusting while unlawfully present
2. Explain that USCIS "portability" rules do not maintain lawful status for purposes of adjustment
3. Inform her that consular processing was a viable alternative to domestic adjustment

These failures constitute deficient performance under Lozada. Ms. Salas suffered prejudice because she would have pursued consular processing and avoided the §245(k) bar entirely. She satisfies all procedural requirements, including notification of former counsel and filing a state bar complaint.

The evidence is overwhelming. Ms. Salas is entitled to reopening of her I-485 and reconsideration on the merits.

## III. LEGAL STANDARD (1 page)

### Purpose
Establish the legal framework that governs your motion. Cite primary authority.

### Structure
1. State the Lozada three-part test
2. Cite each requirement
3. Preview how your case fits each requirement

### Example

---

**III. LEGAL STANDARD: THE LOZADA TEST**

Under Matter of Lozada, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988), an applicant seeking to reopen an immigration proceeding on grounds of ineffective assistance of counsel must demonstrate three elements:

1. **Deficient Performance**: The applicant must show that counsel's conduct fell below the standard of practice expected of immigration attorneys operating in similar circumstances. Counsel's conduct must be "objectively unreasonable given the facts and law available at the time." Matter of Compean, 24 I&N Dec. 710 (BIA 2009).

2. **Prejudice**: The applicant must demonstrate that the deficient performance prejudiced the case—that "but for" counsel's errors, there is a reasonable probability of a different outcome. The applicant need not prove that she would definitely have won, but rather that there is a reasonable probability she would have received a more favorable result.

3. **Procedural Compliance**: The applicant must comply with the procedural requirements of 8 CFR §103.5(a)(4), including:
   - Submission of an affidavit detailing the claim
   - Notification of the former counsel with opportunity to respond
   - Filing within the prescribed time period
   - If applicable, proof of filing a bar complaint or explanation for not doing so

See 8 CFR §103.5(a)(4); Matter of Compean, supra.

The burden is on the applicant to establish these three elements by clear and convincing evidence. However, where an applicant has submitted credible evidence supporting each element, the motion should be granted.

Ms. Salas satisfies all three Lozada requirements.

## IV. ARGUMENT (5–10 pages)

### A. Counsel's Performance Was Deficient

#### Purpose
Demonstrate that counsel's conduct violated professional norms.

#### Structure
1. Define the standard for deficiency
2. Show what competent counsel would have done
3. Present evidence of what your counsel actually did (or failed to do)
4. Explain why the performance was objectively unreasonable

#### Example for Julia

---

**IV. ARGUMENT**

**A. Counsel's Performance Was Deficient**

Immigration counsel has a professional duty to explain material consequences of legal decisions and to advise clients of available alternatives. The errors of Ms. Salas's former counsel fall far below the standard of competence expected in immigration practice.

**1. Standard: What Competent Counsel Must Do**

Competent immigration counsel handling an EB-1A petition with concurrent I-485 filing must:

- Understand and explain INA §245(k) and its bar to adjustment of status for applicants with 24+ months unlawful presence
- Calculate and track the applicant's unlawful presence timeline
- Advise the applicant if the timeline creates risk
- Distinguish between "portability" under INA §204(j), which maintains an I-140 priority date across employers, and lawful status for adjustment purposes
- Inform the applicant of all available processing options, including consular processing (DS-260) for EB-1A beneficiaries
- Avoid affirmatively misleading the applicant about these material matters

These are not advanced or esoteric principles. They are foundational to adjustment law. See INA §245(c)(8); 8 CFR §245.1(c)(2).

**2. What Counsel Actually Did**

The evidence shows that Ms. Salas's former counsel failed on every count.

On or about [DATE], counsel advised Ms. Salas: "[QUOTE FROM EMAIL OR DOCUMENT]. Don't worry about the unlawful presence issue—portability will protect you." [Cite Exhibit A].

This advice was wrong. Counsel failed to:

(a) **Explain §245(k)**: At no time did counsel explain the statutory bar to adjustment for applicants with 24+ months unlawful presence. Ms. Salas's affidavit confirms: "Counsel never mentioned the 24-month rule. If I had known about it, I would never have filed the I-485." [Exhibit B].

(b) **Distinguish "Portability" from Lawful Status**: Counsel conflated two distinct concepts. "Portability" is a mechanism allowing EB-1A beneficiaries to change sponsoring employers without restarting the I-140 process. See INA §204(j)(2); 8 CFR §204.5(j)(1). It maintains the I-140 priority date. It does NOT maintain the applicant's lawful status for purposes of adjustment under §245.

The regulation is clear: "For adjustment of status purposes, an employee may be considered a beneficiary of an approved petition if the employee's priority date is current and the employee has been continuously employed... and remains in valid status." 8 CFR §245.2(a)(2) (emphasis added). There is no "portability" exception to the requirement of valid status.

Counsel's statement that "portability will protect you" was affirmatively misleading.

(c) **Advise on Consular Processing**: Counsel never informed Ms. Salas that EB-1A beneficiaries have the option to adjust status abroad through consular processing (DS-260). Had counsel done so, Ms. Salas and her employer would have pursued this route.

Consular processing for EB-1A is well-established. See INA §245(a) (allowing application for adjustment at a U.S. consulate for visa applicants); DOS Visa Manual, ch. 9 (EB-1A processing).

**3. Deficiency is Objectively Unreasonable**

These omissions and misstatements constitute deficient performance for the following reasons:

- §245(k) is a foundational statutory provision that any competent adjustment counsel must know and explain
- Misrepresenting the scope of "portability" is affirmative error, not mere strategy
- Failing to mention consular processing—an obvious alternative—constitutes failure to explore available remedies
- The failures were not matters of judgment but matters of competence

The Lozada standard does not require perfection. It requires reasonableness. Counsel's conduct here falls below any reasonable standard. Matter of J-E-C-, 23 I&N Dec. 368 (BIA 2002).

### B. Counsel's Deficient Performance Prejudiced Ms. Salas

#### Purpose
Show that but for the errors, the outcome would have been different.

#### Structure
1. Identify the error and its impact
2. Explain the alternative that would have occurred
3. Show that the alternative was viable and available
4. Provide evidence that the applicant would have pursued it
5. Explain why the alternative outcome would have been favorable

#### Example for Julia

---

**B. Counsel's Deficient Performance Prejudiced Ms. Salas**

Ms. Salas suffered clear prejudice from counsel's failures. There is a reasonable probability that, but for the deficient representation, she would have pursued consular processing and would have been approved as an EB-1A beneficiary without encountering the §245(k) bar.

**1. The But-For Scenario**

Had counsel properly advised Ms. Salas on §245(k) and consular processing, the sequence of events would have been different:

- **If advised of §245(k)**: Ms. Salas would have immediately understood the risk of accruing 24+ months unlawful presence during I-485 adjustment
- **If advised of consular processing**: Ms. Salas would have consulted with her EB-1A sponsoring employer about pursuing DS-260 processing in her country of origin
- **Result**: Consular processing, avoiding the §245(k) bar entirely

Ms. Salas would have approved immediately upon reaching an approvable status in the consular queue.

**2. Consular Processing Was Viable and Available**

The alternative scenario is not speculative. Consular processing through DS-260 is the standard mechanism for EB-1A adjustment and was fully available to Ms. Salas at the time.

EB-1A beneficiaries routinely process through consular offices using the DS-260 application form. The State Department has procedures explicitly allowing this. See DOS Visa Manual, ch. 9; INA §245(a).

Ms. Salas's sponsoring employer, [EMPLOYER], indicated its continued support for her EB-1A petition. [Cite evidence]. The employer would have agreed to support consular processing as well. Indeed, consular processing often facilitates sponsorship because it eliminates the risk of the §245(k) bar and simplifies status maintenance issues.

**3. Ms. Salas Would Have Pursued Consular Processing**

Ms. Salas was not an unmotivated actor. She was pursuing permanent residence actively. She retained counsel specifically to navigate the EB-1A and I-485 process. Had counsel informed her that the I-485 path was risky but consular processing was available, she would have pursued it.

Ms. Salas's affidavit states: "If counsel had explained the §245(k) rule and told me that consular processing was an option, I would have chosen consular processing immediately. My goal was permanent residence, not adjustment in the United States specifically." [Exhibit B].

Moreover, consular processing is objectively preferable for EB-1A beneficiaries precisely because it avoids the §245(k) pitfall. A reasonable client, facing the choice between risky domestic adjustment and safe consular processing, would choose consular processing.

**4. The Alternative Outcome Would Have Been Favorable**

The strongest evidence that Ms. Salas would have been approved is her subsequent EB-1A I-140 approval.

On [DATE], USCIS approved Ms. Salas's I-140 petition based on her extraordinary ability in [FIELD]. The approval is conclusive proof that:

- Ms. Salas met the EB-1A standard—an exceptionally high bar
- She was qualified for permanent residence all along
- The only barrier to approval was the §245(k) accrual, which was caused by counsel's failure to advise her of alternative processing routes

An approved EB-1A is the gold standard of proof for EB-1 prejudice. It directly establishes that the applicant was qualified for the benefit all along.

Had Ms. Salas pursued consular processing with her approved I-140, she would have been processed through the consular queue and approved without any §245(k) issue.

**5. Conclusion on Prejudice**

There is more than a reasonable probability of a different outcome. There is near-certainty that, but for counsel's errors, Ms. Salas would have pursued consular processing and would have been approved as an EB-1A beneficiary.

Prejudice is amply demonstrated.

## Formatting and Submission Guidelines

### Page Setup
- **Font**: Times New Roman or Courier New, 12-point
- **Margins**: 1 inch on all sides
- **Line spacing**: Double-spaced (or 1.5 for exhibits)
- **Page numbers**: Center bottom
- **Header**: Include case name and A-number at top of each page

### Length
- **Motion brief**: 5–15 pages (excluding exhibits)
- **Exhibits**: Attach in full; no page limit
- **Total submission**: 20–40 pages typical

### Submission
- **Form**: Print and sign, or e-file if available
- **Copies**: Check service center requirements (typically 3 copies + originals)
- **Fee**: Form I-290B fee ($650 as of 2024; verify current amount)
- **Mailing address**: Service center that issued the original denial

### Checklist Before Mailing
- [ ] Form I-290B signed and dated
- [ ] Motion brief printed, signed, paginated
- [ ] All exhibits included and numbered
- [ ] Certificate of Service included and signed
- [ ] Affidavit(s) signed and sworn
- [ ] Check amount enclosed (if applicable)
- [ ] Cover letter to service center (optional but helpful)
- [ ] Copy retained for your records
- [ ] Proof of service on former counsel and bar association

---

## Common Formatting Errors to Avoid

1. **Wrong form**: Using I-290A instead of I-290B (they serve different purposes)
2. **Inconsistent formatting**: Switching fonts, margins, or spacing mid-document
3. **Missing page numbers**: Making it hard to reference specific pages
4. **Exhibits not labeled**: Submitting exhibits without exhibit labels or numbers
5. **No certificate of service**: Forgetting to include proof of service
6. **Illegible signature**: Using electronic signatures when the form requires wet-ink signature
7. **Incomplete headers**: Not including case name and A-number on every page
8. **Overly long motion**: Exceeding reasonable length (15–20 pages is standard)
9. **Dense paragraphs**: Using wall-of-text format instead of clear, organized sections
10. **No table of contents**: For longer motions (10+ pages), a simple table of contents helps navigation

## Drafting Tips

### Use Clear, Accessible Language
- Immigration law is technical, but your motion should be clear to a non-specialist USCIS officer
- Define terms on first use (e.g., "portability—the USCIS mechanism allowing EB-1A beneficiaries to change employers...")
- Use plain English; avoid unnecessary jargon
- Short sentences beat long ones

### Build Argument Logically
- State a conclusion
- Provide evidence/citation
- Explain the implication
- Move to the next point
- Example: "Counsel failed to explain §245(k). This statute prohibits adjustment for applicants with 24+ months unlawful presence. Because counsel never explained this rule, Ms. Salas did not understand the risk she was taking. This failure was deficient because any competent counsel would have explained it."

### Cite Consistently
- Use proper Bluebook or similar citation format
- Cite to specific pages when available ("at 640" not just "Lozada")
- Parenthetical citations should explain relevance ("citing standard of competence in immigration practice")

### Use Exhibits Strategically
- Don't quote long documents in the motion; cite the exhibit instead
- Put key documents (emails showing the error, approved I-140, bar complaint) early
- Reference exhibits explicitly in the text

### Anticipate Counterarguments
- If former counsel might argue they explained something, include evidence they didn't (emails, timeline)
- If they might argue you should have known better, explain why you relied on counsel's advice
- If they argue prejudice is speculative, emphasize the EB1-A approval as concrete proof of merit

## Final Review Checklist

Before submitting the motion:

1. **Lozada elements**: Does the motion clearly address deficiency, prejudice, and procedure?
2. **Factual accuracy**: Are all dates, names, and facts correct?
3. **Legal accuracy**: Are citations correct? Is law properly applied?
4. **Completeness**: Are all required exhibits included?
5. **Service**: Have all parties been properly served with proof?
6. **Formatting**: Is the document professional and consistent?
7. **Signature**: Is it signed and dated?
8. **Timeliness**: Is it being filed within the deadline?
9. **Fee**: Is payment included and correct?
10. **Records**: Do you have copies of everything for your files?

This motion is your opportunity to demonstrate that you were denied not because you were unqualified, but because your counsel failed you. Make it count.
