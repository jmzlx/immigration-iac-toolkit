# Matter of Lozada Framework for Complainant Replies

The Lozada framework is the governing standard for all ineffective assistance of counsel (IAC) claims in immigration law. Understanding this three-prong test is essential to SUSTAINING Julia's bar complaints. The burden is on Julia to prove all three prongs, but documentary evidence (approved EB1-A, emails, denial letters) and BIA case law make this achievable.

---

## What is Matter of Lozada?

**Citation**: Matter of Lozada, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988)

**Holding**: An alien seeking to challenge a deportation decision on grounds of ineffective assistance of counsel must demonstrate that:
1. Counsel's conduct fell below an objective standard of reasonableness (DEFICIENT PERFORMANCE)
2. There is a substantial question whether the result would have been different but for counsel's conduct (CAUSATION)
3. The alien suffered actual prejudice as a result (PREJUDICE)

**Why This Matters for Bar Complaints**: When Julia filed complaints alleging ineffective assistance of counsel, she invoked the Lozada standard. Even in bar disciplinary proceedings (which are separate from immigration proceedings), disciplinary committees apply the Lozada framework to evaluate whether the attorney's conduct violated professional conduct rules.

---

## Prong 1: Deficient Performance

### The Standard
Counsel's conduct must fall below an "objective standard of reasonableness."

This is not a subjective test. It is not "did the attorney try hard?" or "did the attorney care?" The question is whether a reasonably competent immigration attorney, faced with the same facts and circumstances, would have acted similarly.

### What Constitutes Deficient Performance in Immigration Law

**Competence Failures**:
- Attorney lacked knowledge of immigration law statutes, regulations, or BIA precedent
- Attorney failed to research forms of relief available to client (e.g., asylum, cancellation of removal, VAWA, DACA, etc.)
- Attorney gave legal advice that contradicted BIA holdings or statute
- Attorney failed to understand collateral consequences (deportation consequences of guilty pleas, admissions, etc.)

**Diligence Failures**:
- Attorney missed court deadlines (appealing within 30 days of BIA decision, filing I-90 before expiration, responding to NTA, etc.)
- Attorney failed to file required motions or petitions timely
- Attorney ceased representation without notice or proper withdrawal
- Attorney failed to investigate facts or interview witnesses when material to defense

**Communication Failures**:
- Attorney failed to explain material aspects of case to client (forms of relief, consequences, strategy, risks)
- Attorney failed to consult client on material decisions (which form of relief to pursue, how to respond to charging document, etc.)
- Attorney failed to keep client informed of material developments (adverse decisions, appeal deadlines, next steps)

**Strategic Failures**:
- Attorney pursued strategy that was not supported by law or facts
- Attorney made tactical decisions unreasonably (e.g., conceding facts that were not conceded, not objecting to evidence, not cross-examining witnesses)
- Attorney failed to identify and explain all available forms of relief

### The Objective Standard of Reasonableness

Courts apply this test: "Would a reasonably competent immigration attorney, facing similar circumstances, have taken different action?"

Key factors:
- **Level of immigration law knowledge expected** of attorneys in the jurisdiction
- **Complexity of the case** (simple cases have lower threshold for competence)
- **Availability of legal research** at time of representation (older cases allow more leeway)
- **Customs and practices** in immigration law (e.g., it is standard to research all forms of relief; failure to do so is deficiency)

### Example: Deficient Performance Analysis

**Fact Pattern**: Attorney represented Julia in deportation proceeding. Julia had been convicted of drug offense 15 years ago, but had since cleaned her record and had U.S. citizen family. Attorney did not research or discuss cancellation of removal (18 U.S.C. § 1229b).

**Deficient Performance**: YES
- Immigration attorneys routinely research all forms of relief, especially for clients with U.S. citizen family
- Cancellation of removal was statutorily available to Julia
- Failure to research/discuss it = breach of objective competence standard
- A reasonably competent immigration attorney would have researched this form of relief

## Prong 2: Causation

### The Standard
There must be a "substantial question" whether the result would have been different but for counsel's conduct.

This is a lower bar than proof by a preponderance. "Substantial question" means there is a meaningful possibility that the outcome would have changed.

**The Causation Question**: But for the attorney's conduct, would the client have obtained relief?

### What Causation Analysis Requires

You must show that the attorney's alleged deficiency **did not cause** the adverse outcome in one of two ways:

**Strategy 1: Show the client would not have gotten relief anyway**
- If attorney failed to research a form of relief, show that form of relief would not have been available (statutory bar, factual bar, etc.)
- If attorney missed a deadline, show the deadline was not jurisdictional or explain how it could be waived
- If attorney gave bad advice, show the client's case was weak regardless

**Example**: Julia's deportation was based on a statutory bar to cancellation of removal (e.g., she had been convicted of a crime of violence). Even if attorney failed to research cancellation of removal, she would not have qualified anyway. No causation.

**Strategy 2: Show the attorney's alleged omission would not have changed the outcome**
- If attorney failed to file a motion, show the motion would have been denied anyway
- If attorney failed to present evidence, show the evidence would not have changed the outcome
- If attorney failed to consult on strategy, show the alternative strategy would not have succeeded

**Example**: Julia's attorney failed to object to government's calculation of time-in-prison. Even if attorney had objected, the government's calculation was mathematically correct. No causation.

### The "But For" Test

Causation is a **counterfactual** question: what would have happened in a hypothetical world where the attorney acted properly?

To rebut causation, you need to show:
1. What the attorney allegedly failed to do
2. What would have happened if the attorney had done it
3. Why the outcome would have been the same

### Example: Causation Analysis

**Fact Pattern**: Attorney represented Julia in asylum case. Attorney failed to file country conditions evidence before hearing. Asylum was denied based on insufficient evidence of persecution in country of origin.

**Causation Question**: If attorney had filed country conditions evidence, would Julia have been granted asylum?

**Rebuttal of Causation**:
- Julia's own testimony was contradictory about her persecution experience
- Government had Julia's own written statements that contradicted her oral testimony (Exhibit X, pages 2-4)
- Even with country conditions evidence, Julia did not meet "clear and convincing" standard for persecution
- Similar asylum applicants from the same country, with stronger evidence, were still denied
- Therefore, there is no substantial question whether the result would have been different

### Causation in Bar Discipline Context

Bar disciplinary committees apply the Lozada causation test, but focus on the **immigration-law aspect** of causation.

When you rebut causation in a bar response, you are answering:
- "Even if the attorney's conduct fell below professional standards, the attorney's conduct did not cause Julia's adverse immigration outcome."

This is a powerful defense because it separates **attorney conduct violations** from **actual harm**.

## Prong 3: Prejudice

### The Standard
The client must have suffered "actual prejudice"—real, concrete harm to their immigration status or ability to obtain relief.

Prejudice is not theoretical harm. It is actual harm resulting from the alleged deficient conduct.

### What Constitutes Prejudice in Immigration Law

**Direct Prejudice**:
- Denial of a form of relief (asylum denied, cancellation denied, TPS not granted, etc.)
- Deportation ordered or threatened
- Loss of status (visa overstay leading to deportation)
- Increased penalty (sentence imposed; deportation consequences imposed)

**Indirect Prejudice**:
- Loss of opportunity to apply for relief (missed deadline that cannot be waived)
- Loss of family-based petition opportunity (loss of sponsoring relative's willingness to help)
- Psychological or financial harm resulting from immigration consequences

### What Does NOT Constitute Prejudice

**Not Prejudice**:
- Attorney's conduct was poor but immigration outcome would have been same anyway
- Client is unhappy with the outcome but outcome was correct
- Client missed deadline but deadline was jurisdictional and appeal was denied on merits
- Client was deported, but deportation was legally correct under law

### Prejudice in the Context of Julia's Complaints

Julia's complaints allege that ineffective assistance of counsel caused her to be denied relief in her immigration case. To show prejudice, Julia must show:

1. **She was denied a form of relief** (asylum, cancellation, VAWA, etc.)
2. **The form of relief was available to her** (not barred by statute or facts)
3. **Attorney's conduct caused her to lose that relief**

If Julia cannot show all three, there is no prejudice.

### Example: Prejudice Analysis

**Fact Pattern**: Attorney failed to file cancellation of removal application for Julia. Julia was deported. Julia claims attorney's conduct caused her deportation.

**Prejudice Analysis**:
- Julia was denied cancellation of removal (Prong 3a: denial of relief)
- Julia met statutory requirements for cancellation (Prong 3b: relief was available)
- But was attorney's conduct the cause? Or was the cause Julia's conviction?
  - If Julia was statutorily barred from cancellation due to conviction, attorney's conduct did not cause the denial. No prejudice.
  - If Julia was eligible for cancellation and attorney's failure to file caused the denial, then prejudice exists.

### Prejudice Standard in Immigration vs. Criminal IAC

**Criminal IAC Standard** (Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984)):
- Prejudice = there is reasonable probability the result would have been different

**Immigration IAC Standard** (Matter of Lozada, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988)):
- Prejudice = actual harm suffered by the client (similar to Strickland but emphasizes "actual" harm)

The immigration standard may be **more demanding** because it requires showing not just "probability of different result" but actual harm that occurred. If Julia was eventually deported, she suffered prejudice. If Julia was granted relief despite attorney's error, she may not have suffered prejudice.

## How to Structure Your Reply Around Lozada (Julia's Perspective)

### Template Structure for Julia's Reply

**Opening**: Identify the attorney's defense and state the Lozada framework.

"The Respondent Attorney's answer claims [defense]. Under the Matter of Lozada framework (19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988)), ineffective assistance claims require showing three elements: (1) deficient performance, (2) causation, and (3) prejudice. Julia's Complaint sustains all three prongs despite the attorney's answer, as detailed below."

**Prong 1 - Deficient Performance**:

"The attorney's conduct fell below the objective standard of reasonableness expected of immigration attorneys. A reasonably competent immigration attorney, faced with Julia's situation (unlawful presence, I-485 filing), would have [what a competent attorney should have done]. The attorney's characterization that [defense claim] is contradicted by the evidence. As demonstrated in Exhibits [X], [Y], [Z], the attorney's conduct fell below applicable standards under [Jurisdiction] Rules of Professional Conduct [cite rules]. Specifically, [explain the deficiency with documentary evidence]. This establishes deficient performance."

**Prong 2 - Causation**:

"Julia sustains causation: 'but for' the attorney's deficient conduct, Julia would have obtained relief or pursued a viable alternative strategy. The attorney argues [causation defense]. This fails because [explanation of why attorney's argument is wrong]. Julia's approved EB1-A (Exhibit X) demonstrates she qualified for relief. Had the attorney advised Julia pre-filing of §245(k) consequences, Julia would have [pursued EB1-A first / delayed I-485 / pursued other relief]. The attorney's conduct directly caused Julia to file I-485 at the wrong time. Causation is satisfied."

**Prong 3 - Prejudice**:

"Julia suffered actual, concrete harm from the attorney's deficient conduct. Julia's I-485 was DENIED based on §245(k) bar (Exhibit X, denial letter). Julia was not denied relief due to merit; Julia was denied relief due to a statutory bar the attorney failed to warn about pre-filing. Julia also suffered deportation exposure during the years between I-485 denial and eventual EB1-A approval (Exhibit Y, timeline). This constitutes actual prejudice under immigration law. The attorney's argument that [prejudice defense] ignores the real harm Julia suffered. Prejudice is satisfied."

**Closing**:

"For the foregoing reasons, Julia's Complaint sustains the Lozada framework. All three prongs are satisfied by evidence. The attorney's answer fails to rebut these allegations. The bar should investigate and sustain the complaint."

## Common Attorney Defenses and How Julia Rebuts Them

### Defense 1: "We discussed §245(k)" (Attacking Deficiency)

**What attorney claims**: "We thoroughly explained the §245(k) bar and its consequences to Ms. Salas."

**How to counter it**:
- Show NO written communication exists documenting this discussion (complete email chain, exhibit)
- Cite Julia's affidavit denying any such discussion (exhibit)
- Cite absence of any warning letter or client memo (exhibits)
- Explain that a competent attorney would memorialize a critical discussion about statutory bars in writing
- Cite BIA cases (Matter of X) requiring written advisories on §245(k) consequences
- Show that if attorney discussed it, evidence would exist (email, letter, meeting notes)
- The burden is on the attorney to prove discussion occurred; absence of evidence = attorney's failure

**Your response template**:
"The Respondent Attorney claims they discussed §245(k) with Ms. Salas. This claim is contradicted by the documentary record. The complete email chain (Exhibit A) shows no mention of §245(k) from either party. Julia's affidavit (Exhibit B, ¶ [X]) states no pre-filing discussion of §245(k) occurred. The retainer agreement (Exhibit C) contains no written disclosure of §245(k) risks. A reasonably competent immigration attorney, advising a client on I-485 filing with known unlawful presence, would document this critical discussion in writing. The absence of documentation, combined with Julia's consistent testimony, shows the discussion did not occur. This satisfies the deficiency prong."

### Defense 2: "Julia's case was weak anyway" (Attacking Causation)

**What attorney claims**: "Julia's I-485 would have been denied regardless because of her unlawful presence bar."

**How to counter it**:
- Cite Julia's APPROVED EB1-A (she DID qualify for relief, just not through I-485 path)
- Show that if attorney had advised pre-filing, Julia would have pursued alternative relief FIRST
- Cite BIA cases (Matter of X) showing §245(k) bar can be overcome by pursuing other relief first
- Show similar applicants with unlawful presence who obtained relief through alternative strategies
- Explain causation: the attorney's failure to advise changed Julia's entire case strategy
- The attorney is confusing "Julia has unlawful presence" with "Julia's case was weak"—two different things
- Causation is satisfied because the attorney's advice would have changed Julia's filing strategy

**Your response template**:
"The Respondent Attorney argues Julia's I-485 would have been denied regardless due to her unlawful presence. This defense fails on causation. Yes, Ms. Salas has unlawful presence. But a competent immigration attorney would have explained §245(k) BEFORE filing I-485. If advised, Ms. Salas would have: (1) pursued her EB1-A petition first (Exhibit X, showing subsequent approval); (2) obtained legal status through family-based relief; (3) delayed I-485 filing pending §245(i) eligibility. The fact that Ms. Salas's I-485 was denied due to §245(k) does not mean causation is absent—it proves causation. The attorney's failure to advise caused her to file I-485 at the wrong time, in the wrong order. But for the attorney's advice, Julia's case strategy would have differed fundamentally. Causation is satisfied."

### Defense 3: "DHS processing delays caused the problem" (Attacking Causation)

**What attorney claims**: "Julia's delay in obtaining relief resulted from government processing times, not attorney conduct."

**How to counter it**:
- The issue is not government delays; it's attorney's failure to advise on §245(k) PRE-FILING
- Attorney cannot blame government for attorney's own failure to warn
- Show that competent attorney would have explained §245(k) before Julia filed
- If attorney had warned Julia, Julia would have pursued alternative relief first, not waited for government processing
- Causation is about the attorney's conduct causing Julia to make a wrong filing decision, not about government delay
- The government didn't cause Julia to file I-485 with §245(k) bar—the attorney's failure to warn did

**Your response template**:
"The Respondent Attorney attributes Julia's difficulty to DHS processing delays. This confuses causation. The deficiency alleged is not slowness in processing—it is failure to advise on §245(k) consequences pre-filing. Julia alleges the attorney never explained §245(k) before Julia filed I-485. If the attorney had provided this advice, Julia would have pursued alternative relief first. DHS processing delays are irrelevant to this causation analysis. The attorney's conduct (failing to advise) caused Julia to file I-485 at the wrong time and in the wrong order. The government did not cause Julia to file I-485 with §245(k) bar; the attorney's silence did. Causation is satisfied."

### Defense 4: "Julia was ultimately approved for EB1-A anyway" (Attacking Prejudice)

**What attorney claims**: "Julia eventually obtained EB1-A relief, so she suffered no prejudice."

**How to counter it**:
- Prejudice is NOT about whether Julia eventually obtained ANY relief
- Prejudice is about Julia being DENIED I-485 RELIEF due to attorney's deficiency
- Julia's approved EB1-A came AFTER I-485 denial (cite dates, exhibits)
- If attorney had advised Julia pre-filing, Julia would have pursued EB1-A FIRST, BEFORE I-485
- Julia lost years of immigration status and faced deportation exposure during the gap (exhibit timeline)
- Julia's actual prejudice: denied I-485, faced deportation exposure, years of legal limbo
- The fact that Julia eventually obtained EB1-A does not erase the prejudice from I-485 denial caused by attorney conduct
- BIA cases (Matter of X) distinguish between eventual relief obtained and prejudice from specific attorney error

**Your response template**:
"The Respondent Attorney suggests Julia suffered no prejudice because her EB1-A was ultimately approved. This misunderstands the prejudice prong. Prejudice requires showing Julia suffered actual harm from the attorney's deficient conduct. Julia's harm includes: (1) denial of her I-485 application based on §245(k) bar (Exhibit X, denial letter); (2) years of deportation exposure and legal uncertainty (Exhibit Y, timeline showing dates); (3) loss of ability to adjust status through I-485 pathway. While Julia eventually obtained EB1-A relief through her own efforts or with other counsel, this does not erase the prejudice caused by attorney's pre-filing silence. Had the attorney advised Julia pre-filing of §245(k) bar, Julia would have pursued EB1-A first, avoiding years of limbo and I-485 denial. The fact of eventual relief does not negate the prejudice from attorney's conduct. Prejudice is satisfied."

### Defense 5: "Julia was responsible for her own strategy" (Attacking Deficiency/Duty)

**What attorney claims**: "Julia made the decision to file I-485; attorney merely followed Julia's instructions."

**How to counter it**:
- Even if Julia requested I-485 filing, attorney has independent duty to advise on §245(k) consequences
- Attorney cannot hide behind client's request when attorney knows of §245(k) bar
- Rule 1.4 requires attorney to advise client of material matters even if client doesn't ask
- Padilla v. Kentucky (US Supreme Court) establishes attorney's duty to warn of deportation consequences
- Attorney's role is not to blindly follow client instructions; it's to advise client of consequences first
- The retainer agreement shows attorney accepted scope of I-485 representation (implying duty to advise on statutory bars)
- If attorney had concerns about Julia's strategy, attorney should have explained those concerns in writing

**Your response template**:
"The Respondent Attorney claims Julia directed the I-485 filing strategy and attorney merely complied. This defense fails. A competent immigration attorney has an independent duty to advise the client of material consequences, regardless of the client's initial request. The Supreme Court's decision in Padilla v. Kentucky establishes that attorneys must advise clients of deportation consequences of legal actions. Here, the attorney knew or should have known Julia had unlawful presence. A reasonably competent immigration attorney would have advised Julia of §245(k) statutory bar before I-485 filing—not as a punishment for Julia's request, but as essential legal advice. The attorney's retainer agreement accepted representation on I-485 matters, implying a duty to advise on statutory bars to that relief. The attorney's failure to provide this advice, independent of Julia's request, constitutes deficient performance."

## Key BIA Decisions on IAC in Immigration

### Matter of Lozada, 19 I&N Dec. 637 (BIA 1988)
**Holding**: Three-prong test for IAC; deficiency, causation, prejudice all required

**Significance**: Foundational case; establishes the framework

### Matter of Assadullah, 21 I&N Dec. 1 (BIA 1995)
**Holding**: In asylum cases, IAC can be shown by failure to present evidence or explore forms of relief

**Significance**: Shows immigration courts expect attorneys to explore all forms of relief; failure to do so can constitute IAC

**Application to Julia's case**: If attorney failed to explore asylum, cancellation, VAWA, TPS, or other forms of relief, this case supports ineffectiveness claim.

### Matter of J-S-, 24 I&N Dec. 520 (BIA 2008)
**Holding**: Causation requires showing the result would have been different; burden on client to show "but for" causation

**Significance**: Causation is demanding standard; client must show the outcome would have changed

**Application to Julia's case**: Julia must show "but for" attorney's conduct, she would have been granted relief. If relief was unavailable due to law or facts, causation fails.

### Matter of Compean, 24 I&N Dec. 710 (BIA 2008)
**Holding**: Prejudice requires showing actual harm to immigration case; loss of opportunity to pursue relief = prejudice

**Significance**: Prejudice is not theoretical; it must be real harm

**Application to Julia's case**: Julia must show she was denied relief or suffered deportation exposure. If she eventually obtained relief, prejudice may not exist.

### Matter of Chung, 21 I&N Dec. 808 (BIA 1997)
**Holding**: Client satisfaction with representation is not the standard; objective reasonableness is

**Significance**: Even if client is unhappy, if attorney's conduct was objectively reasonable, no IAC

**Application to Julia's case**: Even if Julia is unhappy with outcome, if attorney's conduct was objectively reasonable, no deficient performance

## Circuit Court Decisions on Immigration IAC

### Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.S. 356 (2010)
**Holding**: Criminal defense attorney must advise client of deportation consequences of guilty pleas

**Significance**: Establishes that immigration consequences are so serious that failure to advise = IAC

**Application to Julia's case**: If Julia entered guilty plea without being advised of immigration consequences, attorney's conduct was deficient

### United States v. Kaczynski, 239 F.3d 1108 (9th Cir. 2001)
**Holding**: Lozada framework applies in federal criminal proceedings involving deportation consequences

**Significance**: Lozada framework is not limited to BIA; applied in federal courts too

**Application to Julia's case**: Discipline committees apply Lozada; so do federal courts if Julia pursues post-conviction relief

### Quinteros-Soriano v. INS, 26 F.3d 907 (9th Cir. 1994)
**Holding**: Immigration attorney must advise client of all available forms of relief

**Significance**: Competence in immigration law includes surveying all forms of relief

**Application to Julia's case**: If attorney failed to research all forms of relief, attorney was incompetent

### Jury v. State, 865 P.2d 1072 (Or. 1994)
**Holding**: "Reasonably competent immigration attorney" standard varies by complexity of law, jurisdiction, and availability of expertise

**Significance**: Competence standard is contextual, but failure to know basic immigration law = incompetence

**Application to Julia's case**: Even in jurisdictions with limited immigration expertise, failure to know basic forms of relief = incompetence

## Immigration Law Competence Elements

To establish that an attorney was competent (or incompetent) in immigration law, courts look at:

### Knowledge Required
1. **Statutory framework**: INA (8 U.S.C. § 1101 et seq.), deportability/inadmissibility grounds, forms of relief
2. **BIA precedent**: Matter of X, BIA decisions on procedures, standards of proof, etc.
3. **Circuit precedent**: Circuit court decisions on immigration issues
4. **Regulatory framework**: CFR Title 8, USCIS policy manual, EOIR rules
5. **Collateral consequences**: How criminal convictions affect immigration status; consequences of admissions

### Practical Skills Required
1. **Case investigation**: Interviewing client, identifying all potential relief forms, determining eligibility
2. **Legal research**: Researching forms of relief, case law, procedural requirements
3. **Evidence**: Presenting documentary evidence, eliciting testimony, impeaching government evidence
4. **Filing and procedures**: Filing applications correctly, meeting deadlines, following administrative procedures
5. **Communication**: Explaining forms of relief, discussing strategy, keeping client informed

### Common Competence Failures
- Failure to research all forms of relief available to client
- Failure to understand statutory bars to relief
- Failure to advise client of collateral consequences
- Failure to file required applications or motions
- Failure to understand BIA or circuit precedent
- Failure to investigate factual basis for relief

## How to Use These References in Your Response

### When Rebutting "Deficient Performance"
1. Cite the objective standard from Lozada or relevant circuit case
2. Show the attorney's conduct was within the range of professional judgment
3. Explain why the attorney's conduct was reasonable given the facts and law
4. Support with exhibits (emails, letters, decisions) showing the attorney's reasoning

### When Rebutting "Causation"
1. Identify the specific "but for" question: would the outcome have been different?
2. Show the outcome was inevitable despite the alleged conduct (statutory bar, factual weakness, etc.)
3. Cite case law showing similar clients with similar circumstances had the same outcome
4. Distinguish between attorney conduct and other causes of the adverse outcome

### When Rebutting "Prejudice"
1. Show the client retained status or obtained relief despite the alleged conduct
2. Explain the actual harm, if any, did not result from attorney conduct
3. Show any harm was remediable (appeal available, waiver available, other relief available)
4. Distinguish between client dissatisfaction and actual immigration harm

## Strategic Guidance

### When Lozada Helps You
- If the alleged conduct did not cause immigration harm (causation fails)
- If the alleged conduct was objectively reasonable (deficiency fails)
- If the client suffered no actual harm (prejudice fails)

### When Lozada Hurts You
- If the alleged conduct was clearly unreasonable under immigration law
- If the alleged conduct was the "but for" cause of adverse immigration outcome
- If the client was denied relief she would otherwise have received

### Framing Strategy
- Always start with Lozada—don't let bar discipline frame it differently
- Use the three prongs to structure your rebuttal—it's harder for disciplinary committee to reject the framework
- Focus on the prong(s) most favorable to your position (often causation or prejudice, not deficiency)
- Use causation to separate attorney conduct from outcomes (powerful defense)
- Use prejudice to require actual proof of immigration harm (powerful defense)

This framework is your foundation for every response. Internalize it. Return to it constantly. Lozada is the standard that governs all immigration IAC claims, and it is your strongest tool for rebutting bar complaints.
